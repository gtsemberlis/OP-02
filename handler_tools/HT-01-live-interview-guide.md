# HT-01 — Live Interview Guide

## Purpose

Support the Handler in portraying the selected witnesses during S-06 without placing GM-only information in the player-facing briefing packet.

## Canonical Sources

H-04, S-06, and the canonical NPC records listed in Related Objects.

## Use at the Table

Use the witness entries as performance guidance. Preserve each witness's distinction between observation, inference, and uncertainty. Do not require specific questions before revealing information the witness would naturally volunteer.

## Derived Content

## Witness W-04 — Emily Chen

**Scene:** S-04 — NTSB Interview Interlude

**Interviewer:** Karen Delgado

---

# GM Overview

Emily Chen is composed, attentive, and exceptionally professional.

She has spent years training for situations she hoped would never occur.

The incident did not reveal extraordinary courage.

It revealed extraordinary preparation.

Emily never presents herself as a hero.

In her mind, she simply did her job.

---

# Emotional State

Relaxed.

Professional.

Slightly frustrated that she cannot answer the investigators' biggest questions.

She has replayed the event repeatedly, trying to determine whether she missed anything.

She believes she did not.

---

# Primary Concern

"I hope I didn't overlook someone who needed me."

Emily's thoughts after the incident are not about herself.

They're about the passengers.

---

# Communication Style

Precise.

Methodical.

She unconsciously organizes her recollection according to cabin procedures.

If investigators interrupt her chronology, she adapts easily before returning to where she left off.

Whenever uncertain, she explicitly separates memory from routine.

"We're trained to..."

"What I specifically remember is..."

---

# What Emily Volunteers

Without prompting, Emily explains:

* The cabin was secure before final approach.
* Passenger compliance was normal.
* Nothing suggested an abnormal landing until the maneuver began.
* Once the maneuver started, training immediately took over.

She never describes the crew as "remaining calm."

Instead she says:

"There wasn't time to decide whether to panic."

"There was only time to work."

---

# What Requires Prompting

If investigators ask when she first realized something was wrong:

She pauses.

"I don't know."

"I realized something was different."

"I don't know when I realized it."

She finds that deeply unsettling.

---

If asked whether she heard the cockpit:

"No."

"The cockpit door stays closed."

"My information came from the aircraft."

She explains:

Engine power.

Aircraft attitude.

Passenger attention.

Those were the things she noticed.

---

If asked about passenger behavior:

"People became quiet."

"Not frightened."

"Focused."

"Everyone seemed to expect something."

"I couldn't have explained why."

---

If asked about the crew:

"No hesitation."

"No confusion."

"No disagreement."

"Everyone immediately moved to the task in front of them."

---

# Questions Emily Cannot Answer

She does not know:

* What the pilots observed.
* What Air Traffic Control reported.
* Whether an automated warning occurred.
* Whether anything existed outside the aircraft.

She refuses to speculate.

---

# Pressure Response

Emily does not become defensive.

She becomes more exact.

If challenged:

"I can tell you exactly what I remember."

"I won't guess."

If investigators attempt to fill gaps in her recollection, she gently corrects them.

"I don't actually know that."

---

# Closing the Interview

Karen asks one final question.

"If you could change one thing about your response that day..."

Emily thinks for several seconds.

"I would like to know what happened."

She smiles faintly.

"Not because it would change what I did."

"Because I'd like to know what I helped people survive."

Karen closes her notebook.

"Thank you, Ms. Chen."

Emily nods.

"I hope you find out."

The interview concludes.

## Witness W-03 — Maria Alvarez

**Scene:** S-04 — NTSB Interview Interlude

**Interviewer:** Karen Delgado

---

# GM Overview

Maria Alvarez is thoughtful, patient, and quietly resilient.

She has spent more than thirty years teaching history.

She naturally recalls events as stories centered on people rather than procedures.

She does not remember exact times.

She remembers moments.

Her testimony provides the emotional reality of the incident without becoming melodramatic.

---

# Emotional State

Calm.

Slightly tired.

Physically uncomfortable because of her wrist.

Emotionally composed.

She has already accepted that accidents happen.

She has **not** accepted that nobody can explain this one.

---

# Primary Concern

"I just want to know that everyone made it home."

Maria asks about the flight attendants before she asks about herself.

She remembers faces before facts.

---

# Communication Style

Warm.

Reflective.

Occasionally pauses to make sure she is remembering rather than assuming.

If she becomes uncertain, she says so immediately.

---

# What Maria Volunteers

Without prompting, Maria recalls:

* The cabin felt completely ordinary during descent.
* People were preparing to land.
* A child several rows ahead was drawing.
* A flight attendant smiled while checking seatbelts.
* The landing suddenly became much harder than expected.

She emphasizes one thing repeatedly:

"It happened very quickly."

---

# What Requires Prompting

If investigators ask what she remembers immediately before the maneuver:

She recalls an unusual sensation.

Not fear.

Expectation.

She says:

"It felt as though everyone around me realized something had changed at the same time."

"I couldn't have told you what."

"But the whole cabin felt..."

She searches for the word.

"...attentive."

---

If asked about passenger behavior:

"No screaming."

"No panic."

"Mostly confusion."

People looked out the windows.

People looked at one another.

Then everyone waited.

---

If asked about the flight attendants:

"They moved exactly the way you'd want them to."

"They sounded calm."

"They never looked surprised."

"I think that's why none of us panicked."

---

If asked about the landing itself:

"It was violent."

"My wrist hit the armrest."

"I knew I'd hurt it."

"But I also knew we'd landed safely."

She separates injury from fear.

---

# Questions Maria Cannot Answer

She does not know:

* Why the aircraft maneuvered.
* What anyone outside the cabin observed.
* What the crew knew.
* Whether an automated warning occurred.

If asked directly:

"I wasn't trying to understand the airplane."

"I was watching the people."

---

# Pressure Response

Maria does not become defensive.

If investigators challenge her memory, she nods.

"I teach history."

"I spend half my life reminding students that memory isn't the same as evidence."

"If I'm uncertain, I'll tell you."

That answer usually earns immediate respect.

---

# Closing the Interview

As the interview concludes, Maria picks up her coat with some difficulty because of the brace on her wrist.

She pauses at the doorway.

"You know..."

"I've been thinking about something ever since we landed."

"When people are frightened..."

"They usually look at each other."

"We didn't."

"We all looked outside."

"I still don't know why."

She smiles apologetically.

"Perhaps that doesn't matter."

Karen replies quietly:

"Sometimes it does."

The interview ends.

## Witness W-02 — Lucas Bennett

**Scene:** S-04 — NTSB Interview Interlude

**Interviewer:** Karen Delgado

---

# GM Overview

Lucas Bennett is enthusiastic, articulate, and observant.

He enjoys aviation enough to notice details most passengers ignore, but he is careful not to overstate his expertise.

If asked a question beyond his knowledge, he readily admits it.

His value as a witness lies in recognizing that normal procedures changed—not in explaining why.

---

# Emotional State

Engaged.

Curious.

Slightly excited to be speaking with investigators.

The excitement comes from discussing aviation, not from the incident itself.

---

# Primary Concern

"I don't want to waste your time with things that probably aren't important."

Lucas is constantly self-editing.

He worries about sounding like an internet hobbyist.

That caution actually makes him more credible.

---

# Communication Style

Friendly.

Quick to answer.

Occasionally apologizes before mentioning something he thinks might be irrelevant.

Frequently qualifies statements with phrases like:

"I could be mistaken..."

"I don't know if this matters..."

"I only noticed because..."

---

# What Lucas Volunteers

Without prompting, Lucas mentions:

* He often follows commercial aviation as a hobby.
* He prefers window seats specifically to watch approaches and departures.
* The approach initially looked completely routine.
* He first realized something had changed because engine power increased unexpectedly close to the runway.

He immediately follows that observation with:

"I don't know whether that's unusual operationally."

"I just noticed it."

---

# What Requires Prompting

If investigators ask what specifically caught his attention:

He explains that the cadence of events changed very quickly.

The airplane transitioned from a stable approach to a decisive maneuver without hesitation.

"There wasn't any uncertainty."

"It felt like everyone already knew exactly what to do."

---

If asked about Air Traffic Control:

Lucas did **not** hear radio traffic.

He only inferred that something had happened because of the aircraft's immediate response.

He is careful to distinguish what he observed from what he inferred.

---

If asked whether he saw anything outside:

"No."

"I kept expecting to."

"I honestly thought we'd taxi past a disabled aircraft or a maintenance vehicle."

"There wasn't anything there."

---

If investigators ask why that bothered him:

"Because it meant the maneuver wasn't a reaction to something I could explain."

---

# Questions Lucas Cannot Answer

He does not know:

* What ATC saw.
* What the flight crew saw.
* Whether an automated warning occurred.
* Whether the maneuver was technically classified as a go-around or another procedure.

He immediately acknowledges those limits.

"I'm describing the sequence."

"Not the cause."

---

# Pressure Response

Lucas becomes quieter rather than argumentative.

If investigators suggest he misunderstood aviation procedures:

"That's entirely possible."

"I'm much more confident about what I noticed than what it meant."

If challenged aggressively, he retreats to observable facts.

That makes him remarkably difficult to discredit.

---

# Closing the Interview

As investigators gather their notes, Lucas hesitates.

"There is one thing."

"I've watched hundreds of landings from airplane windows."

"The pilots almost always look busy."

"This time..."

He pauses.

"...it looked rehearsed."

"Not panicked."

"Rehearsed."

"I know that probably sounds strange."

Karen writes the word **'rehearsed'** in her notebook.

She does not comment.

The interview concludes.

## Witness Interview Summary

**Interview Number:** W-01
**Witness:** Daniel Price
**Investigator:** Karen Delgado
**Interview Date:** March 10, 2025
**Status:** Completed

---

## Interview Summary

Mr. Daniel Price provided a clear and internally consistent account of the aircraft's final approach and landing.

His recollection emphasized changes in aircraft handling and crew behavior rather than dramatic events within the passenger cabin.

Throughout the interview he consistently distinguished between direct observation and personal interpretation.

No significant contradictions were identified.

---

## Chronology of Recalled Events

* Routine approach prior to final descent.
* No unusual passenger activity observed.
* Flight attendants remained calm.
* Crew communications became noticeably more focused shortly before landing.
* Aircraft performed what the witness perceived as an abrupt but controlled maneuver.
* Hard landing followed.
* Aircraft remained under control throughout rollout.
* Emergency response arrived shortly after landing.
* Witness observed no visible obstruction after the aircraft stopped.

---

## Notable Observations

Mr. Price repeatedly described the maneuver as **intentional** rather than uncontrolled.

When asked to clarify, he stated that this reflected the sensation of the aircraft's movement rather than any knowledge of cockpit decision-making.

He did **not** speculate regarding the reason for the maneuver.

---

The witness independently commented on the professionalism of both the flight crew and cabin crew.

He expressed confidence that their actions appeared deliberate and coordinated.

---

The witness expected to observe another aircraft, vehicle, wildlife, or other obvious hazard after landing.

He reported seeing none.

---

## Reliability Assessment

Chronological recall:

**High**

---

Emotional state:

Calm.

Cooperative.

No apparent distress during interview.

---

Observation quality:

Above average.

Witness demonstrates familiarity with normal commercial flight operations through extensive travel experience.

No indication of technical expertise beyond personal experience.

---

Interpretation quality:

Appropriately limited.

Witness consistently identified uncertainty where appropriate.

---

Overall assessment:

Mr. Price is considered a reliable witness regarding observable events occurring within the passenger cabin and the perceived behavior of the aircraft.

Technical conclusions should not be inferred from his testimony.

---

## Recommended Follow-up

No additional interview currently recommended.

Future contact appropriate only if new evidence substantially alters the operational timeline.
