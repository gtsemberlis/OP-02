# Mohawk Valley Community College

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

Mohawk Valley Community College (MVCC)
Where is it?

Mohawk Valley Community College's Utica campus sits just west of downtown, serving thousands of students across Oneida County and the Mohawk Valley.

Modern academic buildings, engineering labs, administrative offices, and student spaces surround landscaped courtyards connected by pedestrian walkways. Students move constantly between classes while faculty, maintenance staff, and administrators keep the campus functioning almost invisibly.

Unlike the isolation of Hogsback State Forest, MVCC is a place built around collaboration and opportunity.

For Samantha Carter, it was where she was building her future.

For Nate Collins, it was where he quietly enabled thousands of other people to build theirs.

What are the sights, sounds, and atmosphere?

The campus feels vibrantly alive.

Students cross the quad carrying backpacks, laptops, and coffee cups.

Faculty gather outside classrooms between lectures.

Maintenance carts move along service roads.

The sounds of conversation, laughter, and distant classroom activity create an atmosphere of ordinary purpose.

Inside the engineering buildings, bulletin boards advertise internships, robotics competitions, amateur radio club meetings, makerspace workshops, and transfer opportunities.

Everything speaks of possibility.

Nothing suggests tragedy.

That normalcy becomes increasingly poignant as investigators realize two members of this community disappeared under circumstances no one here can explain.

Unlike the victims' homes, the campus never stops.

Each semester brings new students.

New schedules.

New ambitions.

Life continues, even while particular absences remain quietly felt.

Dean's Office & Engineering Division

The investigators are directed to Dr. Thomas Edward Sullivan, Dean of Engineering & Applied Technologies.

His office immediately reflects decades spent balancing engineering, education, and mentorship.

Bookshelves hold engineering texts alongside accreditation binders and student project awards.

Framed photographs show graduating classes, faculty, robotics competitions, and STEM outreach events.

A leather portfolio filled with handwritten notes sits on his desk despite the computer beside it.

The office feels welcoming rather than administrative.

Students clearly come here for conversations, not disciplinary meetings.

Since the disappearances, however, two folders appear on his desk more often than he'd like.

One bears Samantha Carter's name.

The other, Nathaniel Collins'.

Information Technology Department

If investigators wish to understand Nate's professional life, Sullivan arranges access to the Information Technology offices where Nate worked.

The contrast with his apartment is striking.

Server rooms hum steadily behind secured doors.

Network racks blink with orderly rows of status LEDs.

Campus maps showing fiber routes and wireless coverage hang beside disaster recovery documentation.

Coworkers describe Nate with remarkable consistency.

Reliable.

Patient.

Technically exceptional.

The sort of employee everyone depended upon but few realized they depended upon until he wasn't there.

His office remains largely untouched.

A backpack still hangs from the back of his chair.

Technical manuals remain open on his desk.

A whiteboard contains unfinished notes for an upcoming infrastructure upgrade.

It feels less like an abandoned office than one waiting for its owner to return.

Who is associated with it?
Dr. Thomas Edward Sullivan

Primary interview.

Warm.

Patient.

Thoughtful.

A veteran educator and engineer who chose community college leadership after retirement because he wanted to work directly with students again. He believes community colleges "create opportunities," and he makes a point of learning students' names rather than treating them as enrollment statistics.

The disappearances of Samantha Carter and Nate Collins are deeply personal to him.

One was a promising engineering student.

The other a respected colleague and quiet mentor.

Unlike Chief Patrick O'Connor, whose instinct is to protect a town, or Elena Alvarez, whose responsibility is the investigation, Sullivan's first concern is the well-being of the people entrusted to his institution.

Faculty and Staff

Professors, advisors, IT personnel, and administrative staff provide consistent portraits of both victims.

Samantha is remembered as exceptionally curious and prepared.

Nate is remembered as the person who quietly solved everyone's problems without seeking recognition.

Neither was controversial.

Neither accumulated enemies.

Neither stood out because of conflict.

They stood out because of competence.

What can be learned there?

MVCC provides investigators with the clearest picture of who Samantha and Nate were before the forest.

Investigators may learn:

Samantha maintained excellent academic standing.
Faculty regarded her as intelligent, engaged, and unusually inquisitive.
After returning from Hogsback, several professors noticed subtle but unmistakable changes in her focus and perception.
Nate had worked at MVCC for years as Senior Network Administrator and was universally respected for his technical expertise and professionalism.
He quietly mentored students who showed genuine curiosity, particularly within the engineering program and amateur radio club.
Samantha and Nate knew one another through entirely ordinary institutional channels: the Engineering Technology program, the campus makerspace, and the amateur radio club.
Nate helped Samantha configure software for a handheld radio, troubleshoot an antenna, gain access to the campus radio shack, and understand packet radio fundamentals. Their relationship was consistently described by faculty and students as professional and appropriate.

This becomes an important investigative revelation.

At first glance, their connection appears suspicious.

Within minutes of speaking to Sullivan, investigators understand it is exactly the kind of mentorship the college encourages.

What initially looks like a lead becomes an eliminated hypothesis.

What artifacts can be found there?

Investigators may obtain:

Samantha's academic transcript.
Class schedules and attendance records.
Faculty observations.
Student support documentation (through appropriate legal process).
Nate's employment records.
Building access authorizations.
Campus network responsibilities.
Amateur radio club sponsorship records.
Makerspace access policies.
Campus radio shack documentation.
Security access logs.
Campus photographs showing Samantha and Nate participating in unrelated academic activities.

Unlike evidence gathered elsewhere, these records explain relationships rather than anomalies.

They answer why people knew each other, not what happened to them.

How does this location change after the investigators visit?

The investigators' questions ripple quietly through the institution.

Faculty begin revisiting conversations they had dismissed as insignificant.

IT staff compare memories of Nate's final weeks on campus.

Dr. Sullivan personally reviews records to ensure nothing relevant has been overlooked.

He also becomes an active ally.

When additional information surfaces, he forwards it without waiting to be asked.

The college itself continues operating normally.

Its people simply begin remembering more carefully.

What do investigators find on a return visit?

The campus remains busy.

New students fill the walkways.

Summer courses continue.

Yet Dr. Sullivan has assembled additional material after speaking with faculty and staff.

Several professors independently describe the same observation.

After Hogsback, Samantha didn't seem distracted.

She seemed more attentive.

One instructor remarks:

"It wasn't that she'd stopped paying attention."

After a thoughtful pause:

"It was like she was noticing things the rest of us never even registered."

Meanwhile, Nate's coworkers offer a different but equally revealing observation.

They noticed no decline in his technical work.

If anything, he became even more methodical after returning.

What changed was his demeanor.

The reserved systems administrator who had always preferred working quietly behind the scenes began initiating conversations, attending faculty gatherings voluntarily, and mentoring students with a newfound ease. Nate himself described it as losing the anxiety he remembered having, even though he could still intellectually recall what anxiety felt like. Rather than treating it as a blessing, he began documenting the change like any other unexplained systems problem.

Standing in Sullivan's office, the investigators are presented with a striking contrast.

Samantha's encounter appears to have heightened perception.

Nate's appears to have removed inhibition.

Neither change resembles trauma.

Neither resembles recovery.

Both suggest that whatever happened in Hogsback altered them in ways conventional psychology cannot easily explain.

Design Notes

Mohawk Valley Community College represents human potential interrupted.

Unlike the FBI Field Office, which catalogs evidence, MVCC remembers people. It preserves the ordinary lives that existed before the operation began: Samantha Carter, the promising engineering student; Nathaniel Collins, the quiet network administrator whose mentorship shaped countless students without fanfare.

Dr. Sullivan is the emotional center of the location. He does not describe suspects, procedures, or forensic evidence. He describes potential. To him, the disappearances are not merely missing-person investigations but interruptions in lives that were still unfolding. His perspective reinforces a central theme of the operation: the greatest loss is not simply that people vanished, but that futures full of possibility were diverted by forces no institution is equipped to understand.

Finally, the college performs an essential investigative function. It dismantles a tempting but incorrect narrative. Samantha and Nate did know each other—but for entirely ordinary reasons. By eliminating that false mystery, MVCC strengthens the credibility of the true one.
