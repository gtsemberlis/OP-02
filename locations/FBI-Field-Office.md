# FBI Field Office

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

FBI Field Office / Evidence Repository
Where is it?

The operation begins inside a secure FBI field office in Utica, New York. While the disappearances occurred across Oneida County, the FBI consolidated the investigation into a temporary major case workspace established within the field office after the fourth disappearance.

Access is tightly controlled.

Visitors pass through multiple locked doors before entering the investigative workspace.

Evidence is stored in a secured evidence room adjacent to the case office.

Unlike the victims' homes, nothing here feels personal.

Everything exists to support an investigation.

What are the sights, sounds, and atmosphere?

The office feels busy without feeling chaotic.

Telephones ring intermittently.

Printers quietly produce reports.

Agents move between offices carrying case files and coffee.

Conversations occur behind partially closed office doors.

The fluorescent lighting is bright but slightly harsh.

Inside the major case room, an entire wall has been dedicated to the disappearances.

Victim photographs.

Maps.

Timelines.

Search areas.

Vehicle movements.

Phone records.

Every available lead has been documented.

None have explained what happened.

Boxes of evidence line one wall.

Several folding tables hold binders, forensic reports, and interview transcripts awaiting review.

The atmosphere is professional.

Disciplined.

Methodical.

Nothing about the room suggests failure.

Only incompleteness.

The investigators immediately understand they are inheriting serious work performed by capable professionals.

The Evidence Repository

Adjacent to the case room is a secured evidence room.

Metal shelving holds sealed evidence containers labeled according to FBI protocol.

Each item has been photographed, cataloged, and logged.

Recovered clothing.

Personal belongings.

Electronic devices.

Biological samples.

Search photographs.

Storage media.

The room is cold.

Quiet.

Everything is arranged with clinical precision.

Unlike the Mi-Go operating chamber, this room reflects humanity's attempt to impose order through documentation.

Every object has a chain of custody.

Every container has a barcode.

Every question has paperwork attached to it.

Who is associated with it?
Special Agent Elena Alvarez

Primary briefing officer.

Analytical.

Patient.

Exceptionally thorough.

Elena is responsible for recognizing that Nate Collins' GPS data could not be reconciled with known physical movement.

She does not claim to understand the anomaly.

She simply refuses to ignore it.

Her professionalism ultimately brings the case to Delta Green's attention.

FBI Major Case Personnel

Several additional agents continue supporting the investigation.

Most appear only briefly.

They are competent investigators who have exhausted conventional explanations without realizing they have encountered the Unnatural.

Their presence reinforces the scale of the original investigation.

What can be learned there?

The field office establishes the factual foundation of the operation.

Investigators may learn:

The complete disappearance timeline.
Search and rescue chronology.
Victim interview summaries.
Cellular analysis.
Financial investigations.
Medical examinations.
Vehicle recovery reports.
Witness statements.
Search maps.
Laboratory findings.

Most importantly:

Every conventional investigative avenue has already been pursued.

The remaining mystery exists not because evidence is missing, but because the evidence contradicts ordinary reality.

Elena can also explain the specific anomaly that triggered Delta Green's involvement:

Nate Collins' recorded GPS track contains positional changes that could not have occurred within known terrain or elapsed time.

The data is internally consistent.

Reality is not.

What artifacts can be found there?

Investigators have access to nearly every piece of official evidence collected during the FBI investigation.

Artifacts include:

Complete case file.
Victim interview transcripts.
Search operation maps.
Cell phone extraction reports.
Medical examination reports.
Recovered clothing and personal effects.
Nate Collins' GPS analysis.
Radio recording supplied by Hal Whitaker.
Search photographs from Hogsback State Forest.
Timeline charts.
Laboratory reports eliminating drugs, toxins, or environmental contamination.
Copies of surveillance footage from nearby roads showing victims entering—but never leaving—the park.

Each artifact represents another conventional explanation that has already been ruled out.

How does this location change after the investigators visit?

Initially, the field office represents certainty.

By sending the investigators into the field, that certainty begins to erode.

As new information returns from interviews, Elena quietly updates the investigation.

Additional notes appear on the timeline.

New locations are marked on the operational map.

Victim statements are annotated with fresh observations.

Unlike the victims, the FBI does not react emotionally.

It adapts procedurally.

The investigation itself evolves.

What do investigators find on a return visit?

The case room has subtly changed.

The evidence wall contains new photographs.

Updated maps now include locations identified by the investigators.

Interview summaries have grown thicker.

Cross-references connect details that previously appeared unrelated.

Elena has begun treating the investigation differently.

She still refuses unsupported speculation.

However, she no longer assumes every anomaly must possess an ordinary explanation.

Instead, she asks increasingly careful questions.

"If the GPS isn't wrong... what assumption are we making that's wrong instead?"

The investigators may also notice another change.

The room is quieter.

Several FBI personnel have quietly been reassigned.

Officially, the disappearances are no longer considered an active federal priority.

Resources are gradually shifting elsewhere.

The Program has inherited the case.

The FBI simply doesn't know it.

Design Notes

The FBI Field Office represents institutional competence confronting institutional limits.

Nothing in this location should imply failure, negligence, or bureaucratic indifference. Every report is complete. Every interview was conducted. Every search was organized. Every laboratory test was performed. The investigators are not discovering overlooked clues; they are inheriting a case in which conventional investigative methods have been applied exhaustively and honestly.

This distinction is central to Episode 2. Delta Green does not exist because the FBI is incapable—it exists because some phenomena remain invisible to institutions built to investigate ordinary crimes. The field office therefore becomes the campaign's strongest expression of another recurring theme: good investigators can reach correct conclusions from incorrect assumptions. The FBI has gathered nearly all of the necessary facts. What it lacks is the conceptual framework to interpret them. That framework is what the Program—and now the player characters—bring to the investigation.
