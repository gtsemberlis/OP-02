# LOC-02 — Hal Whitaker Farm

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

Hal Whitaker Farm
## Where is it?

The Whitaker Farm lies several miles outside the nearest town, connected to the highway by a long gravel road bordered by aging split-rail fences and overgrown hedgerows.

The property has been in the Whitaker family for generations. While no longer a large commercial operation, Hal continues to maintain a modest herd of cattle, a vegetable garden, and several outbuildings. Most of the equipment is older but meticulously maintained.

The farm consists of:

The farmhouse
A detached garage/workshop
Equipment sheds
A weathered red barn
Several fenced pastures
A radio antenna tower rising beside the house

The antenna is visible long before the house itself.

## What are the sights, sounds, and atmosphere?

The farm feels isolated in a comforting rather than unsettling way.

Wind moves through tall grass.

Cattle occasionally call from distant fields.

The slow metallic creak of a weather vane turns with the breeze.

Somewhere, an old AM radio quietly plays country music from an open workshop door.

The property is clean, orderly, and obviously cared for.

Nothing feels abandoned.

Nothing feels haunted.

If investigators arrive during daylight, Hal is almost certainly outside repairing something rather than waiting indoors.

Despite everything that happened, life here has resumed its familiar rhythm.

The only unusual feature is the antenna.

It towers above the farm like an ordinary piece of agricultural infrastructure until viewed more closely.

The Farmhouse

The farmhouse is modest, warm, and deeply personal.

Unlike Samantha's room, which speaks to a future still being written, Hal's home speaks to a life already lived.

The kitchen serves as the center of the house.

A heavy wooden table bears the marks of decades of meals and repairs. A coffee pot seems permanently warm. Local newspapers are stacked neatly beside a pair of reading glasses.

The living room contains worn furniture positioned around an aging television and an impressive collection of amateur radio magazines dating back decades.

Family photographs dominate the walls.

Pictures of Hal's late wife.

Rebecca growing up.

County fair ribbons.

Holiday gatherings.

Military portraits of relatives.

A grandfather clock quietly marks the passage of time.

Nothing in the house feels decorative.

Everything has a history.

The home smells faintly of coffee, old wood, machine oil carried in from the workshop, and fresh bread if Rebecca has visited recently.

It is the home of someone who has spent an entire lifetime in one place.

Who is associated with it?
Hal Whitaker

Primary interview.

Friendly.

Patient.

Practical.

He has no interest in becoming famous for what happened.

He simply wants to understand the strange radio interference that accompanied his disappearance.

Rebecca Whitaker

Hal's daughter.

She does not necessarily live at the farm but visits frequently.

She is deeply protective of her father.

After federal investigators question him, she becomes convinced they are taking advantage of an elderly man who has already endured enough.

Her concern ultimately leads her to contact Chief Patrick O'Connor.

What can be learned there?

The Whitaker Farm provides the investigation's strongest introduction to the radio anomaly.

Investigators may learn:

Hal has been an amateur radio operator for decades.
He carefully documented unusual radio interference both before and after his disappearance.
He believes the interference is more significant than his own missing time.
He noticed patterns but never reached conclusions.
He has experienced no recurring dreams or obsessive desire to return to the forest.

Unlike the other victims, Hal's attention remains focused on observable phenomena rather than memory.

His testimony naturally encourages investigators to begin thinking in terms of signals, frequencies, and instrumentation.

What artifacts can be found there?

Possible evidence includes:

Hal's amateur radio logs covering several months.
Audio recordings of the unexplained buzzing captured on multiple frequencies.
Waterfall display screenshots showing persistent anomalous emissions.
Handwritten notebooks correlating weather, time of day, and radio activity.
Printed frequency charts with unusual observations circled.
Maintenance records for his antenna and equipment.

Investigators who spend time examining these materials begin to appreciate Hal's methodical nature.

Nothing has been sensationalized.

He recorded exactly what he observed.

How does this location change after the investigators visit?

The farm itself changes very little.

Hal continues his daily routine.

The emotional change occurs through Rebecca.

Concerned that repeated interviews are distressing her father, she contacts Chief Patrick O'Connor and questions why federal investigators continue visiting someone who has already cooperated fully.

This introduces subtle tension between local law enforcement and the investigation.

Hal himself remains welcoming should the investigators return.

What do investigators find on a return visit?

The farm remains reassuringly familiar.

Hal is likely repairing equipment, feeding livestock, or sitting in the workshop listening to the radio.

The investigators may notice additional entries in his logbook documenting fresh instances of radio interference.

If Samantha has disappeared again or Kowalski is missing, Hal has already begun monitoring local emergency frequencies out of concern.

He volunteers observations without attempting to draw conclusions.

Rebecca may be present during a second visit.

She is polite but noticeably guarded.

She watches interviews carefully and is more willing than Hal to question the investigators' motives.

The investigators may find themselves spending as much time earning Rebecca's trust as gathering new information from Hal.

Design Notes

The Whitaker Farm serves a fundamentally different purpose from Samantha Carter's residence.

Samantha's home establishes what can still be lost.

Hal's farm establishes how the investigation should be conducted.

Hal is the operation's model witness. He does not speculate, embellish, or seek attention. His radio logs, recordings, and notebooks demonstrate disciplined observation in the face of the inexplicable. They quietly reinforce one of the campaign's central ideas: patterns first, stories later. By grounding the investigators in careful evidence before introducing stranger testimony elsewhere, the farm becomes the intellectual foundation of the investigation as much as a source of clues.
