# LOC-07 — Brooks’ Motel Room

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

Brooks' Motel Room
## Where is it?

Evan Brooks has rented a room at a modest roadside motel several miles from Hogsback State Forest.

He chose it for entirely practical reasons.

It is inexpensive.

It has reliable internet.

It sits close enough to both the forest and New Hartford to allow quick travel while remaining anonymous.

The motel itself caters primarily to traveling contractors, truck drivers, and families passing through Oneida County.

Few guests stay more than a night or two.

Brooks has now been there for nearly a week.

The front desk staff have stopped asking when he plans to leave.

## What are the sights, sounds, and atmosphere?

From the outside, nothing distinguishes Brooks' room from dozens of identical motel doors.

Inside, however, it no longer resembles temporary lodging.

Every available surface has been converted into workspace.

A folding table supports two laptops, several external hard drives, radio equipment, notebooks, rechargeable batteries, and tangled charging cables.

Printed satellite imagery covers the bed.

County maps lie open across the floor.

Sticky notes mark relationships between dates, disappearances, radio reports, and witness statements.

Empty coffee cups compete for space with camera lenses and portable scanners.

The television quietly plays local news with the sound muted.

A police scanner occasionally breaks the silence.

The room smells faintly of stale coffee, printer ink, electronics, and fast food.

It isn't chaotic.

It's simply the workspace of someone who has run out of room.

The investigators may have the uncomfortable realization that, given another week, their own temporary office might begin looking very similar.

Who is associated with it?
Evan Brooks

The room exists almost entirely as an extension of Brooks himself.

Unlike Nate Collins, Brooks documents everything.

He cross-references sources.

Verifies claims.

Archives recordings.

Maintains backups.

He is skeptical of extraordinary explanations, but fascinated by unresolved patterns.

His greatest weakness is not gullibility.

It is a lack of access.

He spends enormous effort rediscovering facts that the investigators obtain through official channels.

Ironically, that independent process occasionally leads him to notice relationships the investigators initially overlook.

What can be learned there?

Brooks' motel room provides a unique perspective on the investigation.

Not because it contains secret information.

Because it organizes public information differently.

Investigators may discover:

A comprehensive timeline built entirely from public records.
Archived news coverage spanning every disappearance.
FOIA requests submitted to multiple agencies.
Satellite imagery comparisons.
Amateur radio logs collected from operators across the region.
Archived SaucerWatch forum discussions.
Missing-person mapping projects.
Weather correlations.
Community rumors sorted by credibility.
Lists of unanswered questions.

Several of Brooks' conclusions are wrong.

Many are speculative.

Some, however, are remarkably insightful.

Investigators quickly appreciate how much progress an intelligent civilian can make using only persistence and publicly available information.

What artifacts can be found there?

The room functions as Brooks' personal archive.

Possible artifacts include:

Spiral notebooks filled with field observations.
Digital voice recordings.
Camera memory cards.
Drone footage.
Portable SDR (software-defined radio) recordings.
GPS tracks from repeated hikes.
Printed topographic maps covered in handwritten annotations.
Motel receipts documenting his timeline.
FOIA correspondence.
Interview transcripts.
Contact lists for amateur radio operators.
Copies of Nate Collins' uploaded material.

Perhaps the most revealing artifact is Brooks' investigation board.

Unlike the Program's evidence wall, it contains every theory Brooks has considered—including those he has already crossed out.

It demonstrates an investigator willing to discard ideas when evidence fails to support them.

How does this location change after the investigators visit?

Whether Brooks realizes they searched the room or simply suspects someone has been there, his behavior changes immediately.

He becomes more careful.

Sensitive notes disappear into locked cases.

Digital files become encrypted.

Equipment begins traveling with him rather than remaining unattended.

He starts photographing his workspace before leaving each morning.

Not because he expects criminals.

Because someone has demonstrated an active interest in his investigation.

Interestingly, this doesn't make Brooks paranoid.

It makes him conclude that his work is important enough for someone to notice.

Depending on the investigators' conduct, this may increase or decrease his trust in them.

What do investigators find on a return visit?

The room has evolved.

Brooks' investigation has become more focused.

The broad speculation that once covered every wall has narrowed considerably.

Entire categories of theories have disappeared.

He has stopped chasing lights in the sky.

Stopped researching local folklore.

Stopped interviewing random witnesses.

Everything now points toward Hogsback.

The forest occupies nearly every map.

The disappearances dominate every timeline.

Radio recordings receive far greater attention than before.

Brooks is converging on the same problem the investigators are.

From a completely independent direction.

If SaucerWatch has fully arrived, the room becomes an improvised coordination center.

Whiteboards track volunteer assignments.

Email inboxes overflow with photographs and eyewitness reports.

Incoming radio recordings arrive faster than Brooks can catalog them.

Video calls connect him to researchers scattered across the country.

The room is no longer simply Brooks' investigation.

It has become the operational hub for a loose network of civilian investigators.

Most of the information is noise.

Some of it is surprisingly valuable.

Brooks spends most of his time separating one from the other.

If Daniel Kowalski has disappeared, the atmosphere changes again.

The investigation board now contains a new photograph.

Daniel's.

Pinned beside the other victims.

Nothing distinguishes him from the others.

That realization is perhaps the room's most unsettling detail.

To Brooks, Daniel has already become part of the same pattern.

He quietly tells anyone who asks:

"I don't think this is escalating."

He studies the board.

"I think we're finally seeing enough of it to recognize what it's always been."

Design Notes

Brooks' motel room represents independent inquiry.

Every major institution in Episode 2 has advantages and blind spots. The FBI has authority. The police have local knowledge. The rangers understand the landscape. The Program possesses historical context. Brooks has none of those advantages. What he possesses instead is intellectual freedom. He is willing to ask questions unconstrained by jurisdiction, bureaucracy, or professional assumptions.

For the investigators, the room should be an uncomfortable mirror. Much of Brooks' work duplicates what they have already done, but it also reveals how the same evidence can be organized into entirely different patterns. His greatest value is not that he knows more than the investigators—it is that he occasionally notices relationships they overlook because they are following institutional procedures.

That makes Brooks an unusually valuable secondary investigator. He isn't an antagonist, comic relief, or exposition device. He is another intelligent human being trying, in good faith, to solve the same mystery. The tragedy is that he is doing so without understanding the danger of getting too close.
