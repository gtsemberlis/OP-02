# LOC-12 — Hogsback State Forest

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

Hogsback State Forest
## Where is it?

Hogsback State Forest occupies several thousand acres of mixed hardwood forest in Oneida County, New York. Crisscrossed by hiking trails, old logging roads, seasonal streams, and rugged hills, it is a popular destination for hikers, hunters, birdwatchers, amateur radio operators, and local families seeking a quiet day outdoors.

Most visitors never travel more than a mile from the main trailhead.

Beyond the maintained trails, however, the forest becomes progressively less structured.

Old logging roads fade into overgrown tracks.

Unofficial footpaths appear and disappear.

Small clearings interrupt dense stands of maple, beech, and hemlock.

Nothing about the terrain feels unusual.

That normality is precisely what makes the disappearances so unsettling.

The forest has no local reputation for danger.

Until this investigation, it was simply another state forest.

## What are the sights, sounds, and atmosphere?

The forest is remarkably ordinary.

Morning sunlight filters through the canopy.

Songbirds call overhead.

Wind moves softly through leaves.

The scent of damp earth, pine needles, and decaying wood fills the air.

Small streams cross hiking trails.

Woodpeckers can occasionally be heard in the distance.

For long stretches, investigators may hear nothing but their own footsteps.

That quiet never feels threatening.

It feels peaceful.

Experienced outdoorsmen quickly recognize the landscape as healthy and familiar.

The unsettling moments come not from what is present—

—but from what is absent.

A breeze suddenly stops.

Birdsong falls silent for a few seconds.

Radio static briefly changes character.

GPS units hesitate before updating.

Compasses remain functional but seem oddly distracting to watch.

None of these observations are dramatic enough to prove anything in isolation.

Collectively, however, they produce an increasingly difficult feeling to ignore.

The forest never appears hostile.

It simply refuses to behave with complete consistency.

Major Areas Within the Forest

Rather than functioning as separate locations, these areas represent recurring investigative spaces that the agents may revisit throughout the operation.

Main Trailhead

The public entrance contains parking, informational kiosks, trail maps, picnic tables, and visitor registers.

Most interviews begin here.

Search teams stage here.

News crews eventually gather here.

It represents the orderly boundary between civilization and wilderness.

Discovery Sites

Each disappearance occurred in a different portion of the forest.

The locations themselves appear entirely unremarkable.

No strange markings.

No burned vegetation.

No unusual geology.

Nothing visually distinguishes them from countless nearby clearings.

Only the accumulated weight of history makes them significant.

Ranger Trails & Service Roads

Poorly marked maintenance roads provide access for forestry equipment and park personnel.

Investigators encounter utility vehicles, conservation crews, and eventually organized search teams here.

These routes become increasingly active as the operation progresses.

Communications Dead Zones

Several low-lying areas consistently produce degraded radio reception.

Cell service becomes unreliable.

GPS occasionally requires additional time to reacquire satellites.

These effects remain within plausible environmental variation.

They are unusual.

Not impossible.

Their true significance only emerges when compared with Nate Collins' recordings and Hal Whitaker's radio logs.

Observation Points

Several ridgelines provide excellent views across the surrounding forest.

Investigators naturally pause here.

They can orient themselves.

Review maps.

Watch helicopters during later searches.

Or simply appreciate how vast the forest really is.

These moments reinforce an important realization.

Almost everything visible appears completely normal.

Who is associated with it?

Unlike previous locations, no single individual defines Hogsback.

Instead, different people interact with it over the course of the operation.

Hikers and campers experience it as recreation.
Rangers experience it as a workplace.
Search-and-rescue personnel experience it as difficult terrain.
Victims experienced it as the last ordinary place they remember.
Investigators increasingly experience it as a landscape full of unanswered questions.

The forest itself is the constant.

Everyone else changes.

What can be learned there?

Hogsback rewards observation more than interrogation.

Investigators may discover:

The geography connecting each disappearance.
Search patterns and terrain features.
Subtle communications irregularities.
Environmental conditions matching Hal Whitaker's observations.
Locations corresponding to Nate Collins' GPS anomalies.
Footpaths frequently used by hikers.
Areas repeatedly visited by search teams.
The practical challenges faced during every rescue operation.

Perhaps most importantly, investigators learn what doesn't fit.

There are no signs of long-term criminal activity.

No hidden camps.

No abandoned vehicles.

No evidence of serial offenders.

Every conventional explanation becomes harder to sustain with each careful survey of the landscape.

The forest slowly eliminates possibilities.

It rarely provides answers.

What artifacts can be found there?

Artifacts recovered from the forest are almost disappointingly mundane.

Survey markers.
Search flagging tape.
Temporary command stakes.
Ranger maintenance markers.
Old trail registers.
Lost hiking equipment.
Discarded water bottles.
Search photographs.
Footwear impressions preserved in police documentation.

Occasionally investigators recover an item belonging to one of the victims.

A snapped camera strap.

A broken trekking pole.

A dropped notebook page.

Each reinforces that something interrupted ordinary activity.

None explain what.

The greatest "artifact" of Hogsback is spatial.

It is the relationship between locations, timelines, and observations gathered elsewhere.

How does this location change after the investigators visit?

Unlike every previous location, Hogsback changes because people change their relationship to it.

As investigators interview witnesses and validate evidence:

Samantha Carter quietly returns to the forest seeking answers.
Christopher Donnelly begins examining infrastructure near the trail system.
Nate Collins' published data attracts outside attention.
Local officers increase patrols.
Rangers revisit previous search areas.
Volunteers discuss rumors of earlier disappearances.

The trees remain exactly the same.

Human activity around them steadily intensifies.

The forest becomes increasingly crowded by people attempting to understand it.

None realize they are all searching for different things.

What do investigators find on a return visit?

Returning to Hogsback always reveals a different forest—not because the landscape has changed, but because the investigation has.

Early revisits are subtle.

Fresh boot prints mark previously undisturbed trails.

Search tape appears in new locations.

Ranger vehicles travel roads that were empty before.

Investigators occasionally encounter hikers discussing the disappearances in hushed voices.

If Samantha has decided to return, investigators may notice evidence that someone has been conducting careful, solitary hikes well beyond the main trail system.

If Christopher has begun his independent inquiry, utility corridors and maintenance routes show signs of recent examination.

If SaucerWatch has arrived, the change becomes impossible to ignore.

Tripods appear at scenic overlooks.

Directional microphones point toward the tree line.

Amateur radio operators string temporary antennas between trees.

Drone pilots search for unusual aerial footage.

People compare maps and speculate quietly over printed satellite imagery.

Most remain respectful.

Many genuinely hope to help.

Some simply want to witness history.

To the investigators, however, the consequence is unmistakable.

The forest is no longer merely a place where disappearances occurred.

It has become a destination.

The mystery itself is drawing people inward.

By the time Park Ranger Daniel Kowalski disappears, that atmosphere changes again.

Professional search teams replace curious visitors.

Helicopters circle overhead.

Search grids divide the woods into sectors.

K9 teams move methodically through the undergrowth.

The same trails that once hosted weekend hikers now carry exhausted deputies, volunteers, and federal personnel working against time.

The forest has not become more dangerous.

It has become the center of an ever-expanding human response to something no one yet understands.

Design Notes

Hogsback State Forest represents the unknown without malice.

Nothing in the landscape should ever behave like a haunted forest or a supernatural realm. The woods are beautiful, ecologically healthy, and entirely plausible. Their power comes from the investigators' inability to reconcile that ordinary appearance with the extraordinary events connected to them.

This location also embodies one of the campaign's foundational design principles: locations represent ways of knowing. Hogsback does not reward interrogation or exposition. It rewards patient observation, repeated visits, comparison with evidence gathered elsewhere, and the gradual elimination of conventional explanations. By the time the investigators finally uncover the Mi-Go operating chamber, the revelation should feel earned—not because the forest concealed a secret lair, but because every prior visit quietly taught them how to see what had always been there.
