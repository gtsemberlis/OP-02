# LOC-10 — New Hartford Police Department

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

New Hartford Police Department
## Where is it?

The New Hartford Police Department occupies a modern municipal building adjacent to Town Hall in New Hartford, New York.

The building serves as the headquarters for local patrol operations, criminal investigations, evidence storage, and emergency coordination. It is large enough to support a professional suburban police agency but small enough that nearly everyone knows everyone else.

Most of the disappearances were initially reported here before state and federal agencies became involved.

To the community, this is where people come when something has gone wrong.

## What are the sights, sounds, and atmosphere?

Unlike the quiet professionalism of the FBI office, the police department feels constantly in motion.

Patrol officers enter and leave through the rear garage.

Dispatch radios chatter continuously.

Phones ring.

A civilian walks to the records window seeking an accident report.

An officer quietly comforts someone filing a theft complaint.

The building feels lived in.

Coffee sits half-finished on desks.

Duty rosters cover bulletin boards.

Children's drawings from community outreach events hang beside department commendations.

This is not simply an investigative headquarters.

It is part of the town itself.

The investigators quickly recognize that these officers don't just police New Hartford.

They live here.

They shop in the same grocery stores as the victims' families.

They coach Little League.

They attend the same churches.

Every missing-person case is deeply personal.

Chief O'Connor's Office

Chief Patrick O'Connor's office is practical rather than impressive.

A large town map occupies one wall.

Another displays photographs from community events, police academy graduations, and department award ceremonies.

A bookshelf contains criminal investigation manuals, FBI National Academy materials, and binders documenting mutual aid agreements.

On his desk sits a neatly organized stack of active case files.

The disappearances occupy the top position.

One drawer remains locked.

Inside are photographs from unresolved cases collected throughout his career.

Not as evidence.

As reminders.

He believes every missing person deserves someone who refuses to forget them.

The office communicates something immediately.

Patrick O'Connor became Chief reluctantly—not because he sought authority, but because he believed leadership was another form of service.

Evidence & Operations Room

A secure room adjacent to the Detective Division contains the department's working investigation.

Unlike the FBI's exhaustive repository, this room reflects an investigation that grew organically.

Large maps of Oneida County are marked with colored pins denoting disappearances, search sectors, witness sightings, and command posts.

Whiteboards preserve timelines from each incident.

Search plans are annotated in dry-erase marker.

Boxes contain volunteer sign-in sheets, K9 deployment records, helicopter flight paths, and mutual aid documentation.

The investigators immediately notice something distinctive.

Many notes are handwritten.

Officers have been updating these materials in real time for months.

The room feels operational rather than archival.

Who is associated with it?
Chief Patrick O'Connor

Primary interview.

Measured.

Professional.

Calm.

A lifelong New Hartford resident, veteran investigator, and graduate of the FBI National Academy, O'Connor never expected to become Chief. He accepted promotion because he believed leadership was another form of public service, and his officers trust him because he protects them while demanding professionalism.

His greatest strength is also his greatest burden.

He refuses to forget victims.

Every unresolved case remains personal.

The five disappearances have become the defining challenge of his career.

Unlike Elena Alvarez, O'Connor has attended the family meetings.

Walked the search lines.

Stood beside parents during press conferences.

He knows every victim by name.

Department Personnel

Patrol officers, detectives, dispatchers, and civilian staff provide additional context.

Each remembers different aspects of the investigation.

Some participated in searches.

Others interviewed witnesses.

Others managed evidence or coordinated mutual aid.

Collectively they illustrate the scale of the local response.

What can be learned there?

The department provides investigators with the history of the investigation before federal involvement.

Investigators may learn:

Original missing-person reports.
Patrol response timelines.
Initial witness interviews.
Search command organization.
Volunteer coordination.
Mutual aid requests.
Search warrant history.
Regional law-enforcement communications.
Evidence chain-of-custody prior to FBI transfer.
How each disappearance affected the town's response over time.

Chief O'Connor also offers something the FBI cannot.

Perspective.

He remembers how optimism gradually gave way to exhaustion.

The first disappearance felt extraordinary.

By the fifth, every officer dreaded hearing another emergency call from Hogsback.

What artifacts can be found there?

Investigators may access:

Original incident reports.
Patrol logs.
Officer field notebooks.
Search maps with handwritten annotations.
Volunteer search rosters.
K9 deployment records.
Mutual aid documentation.
Interview summaries.
Photographs taken during early search operations.
Department radio logs.
Press briefing notes.
Community tip records.

Many of these duplicate information held by the FBI.

What differs are the handwritten observations in the margins.

Small impressions.

Questions.

Ideas that never became formal evidence but reveal how experienced officers were thinking in the moment.

How does this location change after the investigators visit?

The investigators' arrival reassures the department that the case has not been forgotten.

Chief O'Connor quietly instructs detectives to reopen portions of the investigation.

Additional interviews are conducted.

Old evidence is reexamined.

Patrol officers begin paying closer attention to seemingly unrelated calls near Hogsback.

At the same time, outside pressures begin mounting.

Rebecca Whitaker contacts O'Connor after learning that federal investigators interviewed Hal.

She is polite but concerned.

She worries that repeated questioning is harming her husband rather than helping him.

O'Connor listens carefully.

He doesn't interfere with the investigation.

But he now carries an additional responsibility: balancing investigative needs with the well-being of people he has known for years.

What do investigators find on a return visit?

The department feels subtly heavier.

The operations room contains new maps.

Fresh interview summaries have been added.

Detectives have highlighted several observations supplied by the investigators.

Chief O'Connor greets them warmly but looks more tired than before.

He sleeps less.

The strain shows in small ways rather than dramatic ones.

As he reviews the updated investigation, he pauses before one wall covered in photographs.

"I've worked murders."

A long silence.

"You eventually understand what happened."

Another pause.

"This..."

He studies the map for a moment.

"...doesn't want to be understood."

It is perhaps the closest he has ever come to admitting that the case may lie beyond ordinary policing.

He doesn't surrender his professionalism.

He simply acknowledges that professionalism has reached its limits.

If Park Ranger Daniel Kowalski has disappeared...

The atmosphere changes dramatically.

The department is no longer reviewing a completed investigation.

It is managing an unfolding emergency.

The Operations Room has transformed into a county-wide incident command center.

Fresh search grids cover the walls.

Helicopter assignments, K9 deployments, volunteer coordination, and mutual aid requests are updated continuously.

Dispatch traffic has become almost constant.

No one is sitting down for very long.

The investigators immediately recognize the difference between investigating a mystery and trying to save a life before time runs out.

If SaucerWatch has arrived in Oneida County...

The burden on the department has shifted again.

The disappearances are no longer solely a law enforcement problem.

They have become a public phenomenon.

Patrol officers report repeated trespassing complaints around Hogsback State Forest.

Deputies spend increasing amounts of time redirecting curious civilians away from active search areas.

Reporters have begun asking unusual questions.

Emergency dispatch receives calls from people claiming to have seen lights in the forest, strange aircraft, mysterious radio transmissions, or "the same thing that happened to Nate."

Nearly all of the reports are sincere.

Very few are useful.

Evidence technicians now spend as much time eliminating misinformation as they do processing legitimate leads.

Chief O'Connor doesn't express anger toward the visitors.

Only frustration.

"Most of them mean well."

He sighs.

"But every person wandering into those woods is one more person my officers have to keep track of."

Pinned to one corner of the operations board is a growing list titled:

Civilian Contacts

It includes journalists.

Podcast hosts.

Independent researchers.

Drone operators.

Social media personalities.

Amateur radio operators.

And, prominently,

Evan Brooks — SaucerWatch

No crimes are associated with the list.

It simply represents people the department now knows are actively pursuing the case.

For the investigators, the implication is obvious.

The operation is no longer contained.

Every new interview, every uploaded recording, every rumor shared online has created consequences that extend well beyond New Hartford.
