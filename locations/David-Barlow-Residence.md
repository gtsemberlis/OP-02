# David Barlow Residence

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

David Allen Barlow Residence
Where is it?

David lives alone in a modest suburban ranch house on a quiet residential street in Rome, New York, approximately twenty minutes from Hogsback State Forest.

The neighborhood consists of well-kept middle-class homes built in the 1960s and 70s. Lawns are neatly maintained, driveways contain practical vehicles, and neighbors know one another by name. It is the sort of place where unusual activity is quickly noticed but rarely discussed openly.

Nothing about David's house initially stands out except that the blinds are usually drawn, even during daylight.

What are the sights, sounds, and atmosphere?

The house feels occupied but withdrawn.

The lawn is trimmed.

The flower beds have been weeded.

The porch has recently been swept.

Inside, the television quietly plays financial news, local weather, or cable news programming without anyone really paying attention.

Everything is meticulously organized.

Mail has been sorted into neat piles.

Kitchen counters are spotless.

Furniture is carefully arranged.

The air smells faintly of coffee and furniture polish.

There is no dust.

No clutter.

No unfinished projects.

The entire home feels like someone trying to maintain absolute control over an increasingly uncertain world.

Conversation never quite relaxes.

David lowers his voice without realizing it.

He occasionally glances toward the windows before answering questions.

Simple conversations feel oddly procedural, as though he is unconsciously evaluating every answer before speaking.

The investigators quickly realize they are interviewing someone who is frightened—but not of the thing they are investigating.

David's Study

David spends much of his time in a converted spare bedroom that now serves as a home office.

The room reflects his personality immediately.

Everything has a place.

A sturdy desk supports a desktop computer with dual monitors.

One monitor usually displays spreadsheets, banking information, or financial software.

The other cycles between local news, weather, or government websites.

A filing cabinet occupies one wall.

Each drawer is carefully labeled.

Taxes
Insurance
Employment
Home Maintenance
Medical Records

A wall calendar contains handwritten reminders crossing back several years.

Every appointment has been checked off in precise black ink.

Above the desk hangs a corkboard holding utility bills, maintenance schedules, emergency contacts, and warranty paperwork.

Nothing suggests conspiracy.

Nothing suggests obsession.

The room belongs to a practical man who believes organization solves problems.

Only recently have subtle changes appeared.

One drawer now contains a folder labeled:

Missing Time

Inside are newspaper clippings, printed internet articles, handwritten timelines, insurance correspondence, and notes attempting to reconstruct the days surrounding his disappearance.

The folder looks strangely out of place among decades of ordinary paperwork.

Who is associated with it?
David Allen Barlow

Primary interview.

Polite.

Reserved.

Methodical.

David genuinely wants to cooperate.

He simply believes the investigators are asking the wrong questions.

Unable to accept that something fundamentally impossible happened to him, he convinces himself that federal investigators must actually be examining some unrelated issue—his taxes, finances, employment records, or insurance claims.

His fear is entirely genuine.

His explanation is entirely wrong.

What can be learned there?

David provides an important psychological perspective on the investigation.

Investigators may learn:

He remembers almost nothing of his disappearance.
He has become increasingly uncomfortable with repeated federal interviews.
He believes there must be an ordinary explanation hidden beneath the official questions.
He has experienced subtle behavioral changes but attributes them entirely to stress.
He has no interest in returning to Hogsback State Forest.
He has begun reviewing years of personal records searching for a mistake that does not exist.

David demonstrates how an otherwise rational person can construct a completely mundane explanation for an experience that refuses to fit ordinary reality.

What artifacts can be found there?

Investigators may discover:

Correspondence with insurance companies regarding his disappearance.
Employment records documenting his absence from work.
A handwritten notebook attempting to reconstruct the missing days.
Bank statements repeatedly reviewed for suspicious transactions.
Newspaper coverage of the disappearances collected in chronological order.
A personal timeline comparing his disappearance with the other victims.
The "Missing Time" folder containing every piece of documentation David has assembled since returning home.

Unlike Hal's records, David's documents are not evidence gathering.

They are reassurance.

Each document represents another attempt to prove that something ordinary happened.

How does this location change after the investigators visit?

The interview unintentionally reinforces David's growing anxiety.

Repeated questioning convinces him that investigators are quietly pursuing an unrelated matter.

He begins reviewing years of financial records.

He contacts his accountant.

He rereads insurance paperwork.

He searches for discrepancies that do not exist.

He becomes less willing to answer questions—not because he knows anything, but because he fears saying the wrong thing.

The house itself remains immaculate.

Only the atmosphere changes.

What do investigators find on a return visit?

The difference is immediately noticeable.

The curtains remain closed throughout the day.

The television volume has been turned up.

Stacks of paperwork now occupy the dining room table.

Several cardboard archive boxes have appeared, carefully labeled:

Taxes
Employment
Banking
Insurance
Medical

David apologizes repeatedly.

"I'm sorry... I just don't know what it is you're really looking for."

He answers questions more cautiously than before.

Long pauses precede even simple responses.

He occasionally asks investigators to repeat questions, as though trying to determine what they actually mean.

One new detail stands out.

A well-maintained revolver is now within easy reach.

It may rest in a holster on his belt while working around the house, or lie quietly on an end table beside his chair.

David never reaches for it.

He never threatens the investigators.

Its presence is almost incidental.

If asked about it, he shrugs.

"Been sleeping with it nearby lately."

If pressed further, he hesitates before answering.

"Just... doesn't feel right lately."

He cannot explain what he means.

The investigators quickly realize the weapon is not intended for them.

David is preparing himself against a danger he cannot identify.

That realization is considerably more unsettling than open hostility would have been.

Design Notes

David's residence represents the human need to rationalize the irrational.

Unlike Hal Whitaker, who responds by carefully documenting observable facts, or Samantha Carter, who becomes determined to seek answers, David responds by constructing a comforting explanation that fits within his existing understanding of the world. His meticulously organized home becomes a visual metaphor for that effort. Every labeled folder, balanced account, and carefully filed receipt reflects an attempt to restore order after an experience that fundamentally defied it.

His growing hypervigilance should never be portrayed as villainy or instability. The revolver on a second visit is not the symbol of an armed antagonist—it is the quiet admission that, somewhere beneath all of David's rationalizations, part of him knows the world is no longer as safe or comprehensible as it once seemed. He cannot articulate that truth, so he prepares for it in the only way he knows how.
