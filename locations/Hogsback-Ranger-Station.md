# LOC-13 — Hogsback Ranger Station

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

Hogsback State Forest Ranger Station
## Where is it?

The Hogsback State Forest Ranger Station sits just beyond the main public entrance, positioned where paved roads give way to gravel service routes leading into the forest.

The modest building serves as the operational headquarters for park rangers responsible for thousands of acres of state land.

From here, rangers coordinate trail maintenance, wildlife management, search-and-rescue operations, controlled burns, visitor assistance, and emergency response.

Several utility buildings surround the station, including equipment sheds, vehicle bays, and a communications tower supporting ranger radio traffic.

Unlike the trailhead, the station is clearly a workplace rather than a visitor destination.

It exists to support the people responsible for caring for the forest.

## What are the sights, sounds, and atmosphere?

The station is practical.

Mud-covered pickup trucks and utility vehicles occupy the parking area.

ATVs sit beside trailers loaded with chainsaws, pumps, and rescue equipment.

Inside, maps cover nearly every available wall.

Trail conditions.

Fire history.

Wildlife management zones.

Timber surveys.

Search sectors from previous missing-person investigations.

Handheld radios continuously exchange brief, professional conversations between rangers working throughout the forest.

The smell of wet boots, coffee, gasoline, pine needles, and chain oil hangs faintly in the air.

Everything is organized.

Nothing is polished.

This is a place built by people who spend most of their day outdoors.

The investigators immediately recognize another important theme of the operation.

The people here know the forest intimately.

If they cannot explain what happened, it isn't because they failed to pay attention.

Who is associated with it?
Park Ranger Daniel Kowalski

Before his disappearance, Daniel serves as the investigators' principal guide to Hogsback.

Patient.

Practical.

Observant.

He possesses the quiet confidence common to experienced outdoorsmen.

He does not romanticize the forest.

Nor does he fear it.

To Daniel, the woods are a working environment deserving respect rather than mythology.

He knows nearly every trail, stream crossing, maintenance road, and seasonal change within the forest.

His greatest investigative contribution is context.

He can immediately distinguish between something unusual and something merely unfamiliar to city investigators.

His confidence makes later events considerably more unsettling.

If Daniel cannot make sense of the forest anymore, neither can anyone else.

Other Rangers

Additional conservation personnel continue routine operations regardless of the investigation.

They maintain trails.

Assist lost hikers.

Monitor wildlife.

Support search operations.

Most have worked together for years.

The disappearance of one of their own affects them differently than previous missing-person cases.

Daniel isn't simply another victim.

He's family.

What can be learned there?

The Ranger Station teaches investigators how professionals understand Hogsback.

Investigators may learn:

Historical trail development.
Seasonal environmental changes.
Wildlife movement patterns.
Areas frequently visited by hikers.
Terrain difficult for search teams.
Locations of previous rescue operations.
Search methodologies.
Communications coverage throughout the forest.
Which environmental anomalies have ordinary explanations—and which do not.

Daniel is particularly valuable because he gently corrects assumptions.

When investigators point toward something unusual, he often explains it perfectly.

Animal behavior.

Weather.

Topography.

Old logging activity.

Eventually, however, the number of observations without satisfactory explanations begins to grow.

Daniel notices this before he admits it aloud.

What artifacts can be found there?

The Ranger Station preserves decades of institutional memory.

Investigators may access:

Historical trail maps.
Forestry surveys.
Wildlife census reports.
Search-and-rescue documentation.
Missing-person search plans.
Fire history maps.
Maintenance logs.
Ranger patrol journals.
Weather observations.
Communications logs.
Historical aerial photography.
Visitor incident reports.

Many records stretch back years or even decades.

Collectively they demonstrate something important.

The forest itself has remained remarkably stable.

The disappearances have not.

How does this location change after the investigators visit?

Following the investigators' arrival, the ranger staff begins revisiting assumptions that had quietly solidified over months of unsuccessful searching.

Old patrol notes are reread.

Historical maps are compared.

Additional time is spent checking locations previously considered low priority.

Daniel becomes increasingly engaged with the investigation.

He accompanies investigators into the forest when schedules permit.

He quietly begins conducting his own observations during routine patrols.

He never says he believes something impossible is occurring.

Instead, he starts asking increasingly careful questions.

Questions professional rangers rarely need to ask.

What do investigators find on a return visit?

The Ranger Station is almost unrecognizable.

Daniel Kowalski is gone.

His truck remains parked outside.

His coffee mug still sits beside a stack of unfinished paperwork.

His field notebook lies where he last left it.

Nobody has moved any of it.

Not intentionally.

The atmosphere has changed from quiet professionalism to restrained grief.

Rangers continue performing their duties because they have no alternative.

But conversations stop when Daniel's name comes up.

Search maps now dominate the operations room.

Fresh grease-pencil markings cover transparent overlays.

Volunteer assignments.

Helicopter flight paths.

K9 deployments.

Probable search sectors.

Everything now revolves around finding one of their own.

The investigators experience something they have not encountered elsewhere.

For the first time, the people guiding them through the forest have become victims of it.

One veteran ranger quietly summarizes what everyone else is thinking.

"Visitors get lost."

He studies Daniel's photograph for a long moment.

"Danny didn't."

That simple observation carries enormous weight.

Everyone in the building knows there is no plausible scenario in which Daniel accidentally wandered off a trail he had walked hundreds of times.

If SaucerWatch has begun arriving in Oneida County, the station faces an additional burden.

Rangers spend increasing amounts of time redirecting civilians away from restricted search sectors, responding to unauthorized drone flights, and locating amateur investigators who have ventured well beyond marked trails.

Incident boards now include not only search assignments but public-safety concerns related to unauthorized visitors.

No one resents the volunteers.

Many genuinely want to help.

But every unnecessary rescue, every trespassing complaint, and every civilian wandering into dense forest diverts personnel from the increasingly urgent search for Daniel.

Design Notes

The Ranger Station represents professional knowledge of the natural world.

Where the FBI understands evidence, the police understand communities, and MVCC understands people, the rangers understand place. Their expertise establishes the operation's environmental baseline. When experienced professionals who have spent decades reading forests begin admitting that they no longer understand what they are seeing, the investigators appreciate that something genuinely unprecedented is unfolding.

Daniel Kowalski serves another crucial narrative function. Early in the operation, he is the investigators' guide through Hogsback, translating unfamiliar terrain into understandable patterns. His disappearance is therefore more than the loss of another victim—it is the loss of the operation's most trusted interpreter of the landscape. When the person who knows the forest best can no longer find his way home, the investigators understand that they are confronting a problem no amount of ordinary fieldcraft can solve. That realization naturally sets the stage for the extended navigation challenge leading to the Mi-Go operating chamber, where success depends not on following a hidden trail but on synthesizing everything learned throughout the investigation into a new way of reading the forest.
