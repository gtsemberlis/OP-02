# A-09 — Daniel Kowalski Final Radio Transmission

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

Artifact: Daniel Kowalski's Final Radio Transmission
## Description

A ten-second audio recording automatically archived by the New York State Park communications system.

The recording captures Daniel Kowalski's final successful radio transmission before all contact is lost.

Audio quality is excellent.

The recording contains no distortion beyond normal radio compression.

Background audio includes:

Wind moving through dense forest.
Boots crunching over leaves and gravel.
A few distant birds.
Daniel's measured breathing as he walks.

His voice remains calm, professional, and completely unhurried.

Nothing about the recording initially suggests that anything is wrong.

## Who Possesses It?

The original recording is retained within the New York State Park communications archive.

Copies are available through official law enforcement channels.

Brooks eventually acquires an unofficial copy through local contacts if his investigation progresses far enough.

## Where Is It Found?

The recording is stored within the park communications system.

Investigators typically obtain it from dispatch logs at the Ranger Station or through cooperating law enforcement.

## Initial Interpretation

The transmission appears entirely routine.

Dispatch has asked Ranger Three to investigate a report of unusual radio interference received from another ranger several miles away.

Daniel responds while already traveling toward the area.

The recording captures his final check-in.

"Dispatch, Ranger Three... I'm going to check something out. Stand by."

Pause

Dispatch: "Ranger Three, radio check."

A brief burst of static.

Kowalski: "Go ahead."

Dispatch: "Still getting interference up your way?"

A pause. Wind across the microphone.

Kowalski: "...Yeah."

"Nothing obvious."

A few footsteps.

"Signal's acting strange again."

Three more slow footsteps.

"...Hang on."

A pause of nearly two seconds.

"That's..."

Silence.

A soft carrier hiss continues for approximately one second before the transmission abruptly terminates.

No further communications are received.

Repeated attempts by dispatch to raise Kowalski go unanswered.

## Further Examination
Audio Engineering

The recording is authentic.

No evidence of editing, manipulation, or data corruption is present.

The abrupt ending reflects the original transmission exactly as archived.

Communications Analysis

Daniel follows normal park radio procedure throughout the transmission.

Nothing in his wording suggests urgency or distress.

The transmission simply stops.

Whether Daniel released the push-to-talk switch voluntarily or the transmission was interrupted externally cannot be determined.

Signal Analysis

During the final three seconds, faint structured interference becomes audible beneath the normal radio traffic.

The interference matches the same anomalous signal documented elsewhere during the investigation.

Compared with Hal Whitaker's recording, it is weaker.

Compared with Daniel's recovered handheld radio, it represents an earlier encounter with the same phenomenon.

Audio Enhancement

Repeated filtering reveals increasingly subtle environmental details.

Wind.

Leaves shifting.

A distant stream.

Daniel slowing his pace.

At maximum enhancement, investigators may believe they hear an additional faint sound immediately after Daniel says:

"That's..."

Some listeners describe it as metal.

Others describe it as stone.

Others hear nothing unusual at all.

No enhancement produces a definitive interpretation.

The recording stubbornly refuses to reveal exactly what interrupted him.

Psychology

Daniel's voice never rises.

He never sounds frightened.

The hesitation before his final words suggests curiosity rather than fear.

Whatever caught his attention appears to have interrupted an observation—not triggered panic.

## Relationships

The recording directly complements:

Daniel Kowalski's recovered radio.
Hal Whitaker's radio recording.
Nathaniel Collins' signal analysis.
Samantha Carter's field observations.
Brooks' comparative investigations.

Together they establish that the anomalous radio signal consistently precedes close encounters with the phenomenon.

The recording also anchors Daniel's final known movements immediately before his disappearance.

## Investigation Impact

Daniel's final transmission provides the investigation's most reliable timeline for his disappearance.

Unlike witness testimony, every second of the recording is objectively documented.

Its greatest contribution is what investigators eventually realize is missing.

There is:

no fear,
no struggle,
no warning,
no indication that Daniel believed himself to be in danger.

Instead, the recording suggests that victims remain engaged in ordinary observation until the final moments before contact.

His unfinished sentence becomes increasingly significant.

Investigators naturally replay the final seconds, attempting to determine what Daniel was about to describe.

No answer ever emerges.

The recording instead reinforces one of the operation's central themes:

Observation always precedes understanding.

## Changes Over Time

The recording itself never changes.

Its meaning does.

Early in the investigation it sounds like an uneventful status report.

After investigators recover Daniel's radio, interview victims, examine Brooks' archive, and begin understanding the recurring signal, the recording transforms into one of the clearest demonstrations of how abruptly ordinary reality gives way to the Unnatural.

Every replay raises the same unanswered question:

What did Daniel see?

## Handler Notes

Daniel Kowalski's final transmission should never become a hidden-message puzzle.

There is no secret phrase waiting to be decoded.

No monster noise hidden beneath the audio.

No final revelation.

The investigators should replay the recording repeatedly because it feels like it ought to contain an answer.

It never does.

Its emotional weight comes from dramatic irony.

The players know they are listening to the final ten seconds of a good man's ordinary working day.

Daniel does not die heroically.

He does not uncover the truth.

He simply notices something unexpected and begins to describe it.

He never gets the chance to finish.

That unfinished sentence should linger with the players long after the operation ends.

"...That's..."

It is perhaps the closest anyone in the operation comes to seeing the Unnatural clearly—and even then, language fails before the recording does.
