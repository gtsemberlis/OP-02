# SaucerWatch Discussion Thread

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

Artifact: SaucerWatch Discussion Thread
Description

The SaucerWatch discussion thread is not a single webpage but an evolving collection of online discussions spanning multiple platforms.

Although centered on a lengthy Reddit discussion initiated after Nathaniel Collins shares aspects of his investigation with SaucerWatch, the conversation rapidly spreads across related communities devoted to:

UFOs
Amateur radio
Missing persons
Hiking and outdoors
Local New York communities
Police scanners
Conspiracy theories

Hundreds of users participate.

Some contribute firsthand observations.

Others repost local news.

Many speculate wildly.

Within days, factual reporting, rumor, satire, trolling, sincere investigation, and deliberate fabrication become almost impossible to distinguish.

Unlike most artifacts in the operation, this one has no fixed physical location.

It exists only online.

Investigators encounter it through independent internet research, open-source intelligence gathering, or by following references found elsewhere in Brooks' investigation.

The discussion continues evolving throughout the operation.

Who Possesses It?

No one.

The thread belongs to the internet.

Brooks actively participates under his SaucerWatch account and archives significant discussions before they disappear, but hundreds of unrelated users contribute independently.

Deleted posts, moderator actions, edited comments, and reposts mean no two investigators necessarily see the discussion in exactly the same state.

Where Is It Found?

The discussion is discovered through ordinary internet research.

Searches involving:

the disappearances,
amateur radio,
unexplained interference,
SaucerWatch,
missing hikers,
unusual lights,
or related historical incidents

quickly lead investigators toward the thread.

Brooks may also direct cooperative investigators toward specific discussions he believes deserve attention.

Initial Interpretation

The thread initially appears almost worthless.

Investigators encounter exactly what they would expect from a rapidly growing internet discussion surrounding a mysterious event.

The conversation is dominated by:

unsupported speculation,
conspiracy theories,
rumors repeated as fact,
deliberate misinformation,
harassment directed toward victims and their families,
arguments between competing fringe communities,
fabricated eyewitness accounts,
obvious trolls,
users seeking attention,
well-intentioned but inaccurate amateur analysis.

Many posters confidently explain events they know nothing about.

Others attempt to identify victims, speculate about government involvement, or encourage increasingly intrusive behavior toward local residents.

At first glance, the discussion appears to generate far more confusion than useful information.

Further Examination
Open Source Intelligence (OSINT)

A systematic review reveals that, despite overwhelming noise, independent witnesses occasionally report observations before they become public knowledge.

Timeline analysis can distinguish firsthand accounts from later rumor propagation.

Several posts prove surprisingly reliable once compared against known facts.

Anthropology / Sociology

The thread becomes a case study in collective sense-making.

Participants consistently interpret incomplete evidence through preexisting belief systems.

Users advocating extraterrestrial explanations, government conspiracies, psychological explanations, military experiments, or simple coincidence all select evidence supporting their preferred worldview while dismissing contradictory information.

The investigators witness misinformation emerging organically rather than through deliberate manipulation.

Computer Science

Archived versions of deleted posts, account histories, timestamps, and metadata allow investigators to identify which information originated independently and which simply spread through repetition.

The earliest posts often prove the most valuable.

Psychology

The discussion illustrates how uncertainty encourages confidence rather than caution.

The less information participants possess, the more certain many become.

Witnesses who express uncertainty frequently receive less attention than users offering dramatic explanations.

Comparative Analysis

Careful cross-referencing uncovers several isolated discussions predating the Utica disappearances.

Most appear unrelated.

A handful warrant closer attention.

Among them:

A user describing a cousin who disappeared under remarkably similar circumstances in rural Colorado during 1989.

An amateur radio operator in northern Michigan describing persistent HF interference shortly before a cluster of unexplained disappearances in the early 2000s.

A discussion from Arizona documenting unusual radio buzzing reported independently by several operators before another unexplained missing persons investigation.

None of these discussions identify the current phenomenon.

None even agree on what occurred.

Yet each independently preserves small fragments of observations strikingly similar to evidence collected during the current investigation.

Investigation Impact

The SaucerWatch thread demonstrates both the strengths and weaknesses of collective intelligence.

Unlike Brooks, who evaluates evidence systematically, the online community generates information through sheer volume.

Most of that information is wrong.

Some of it is fabricated.

Some simply repeats existing rumors.

Yet buried within thousands of posts are genuine firsthand observations that would likely never appear in official investigations.

The investigators gradually realize that the thread functions less as a source of answers than as an uncontrolled archive of human observation.

Individuals across the country independently report:

unusual radio interference,
isolated disappearances,
strange environmental sensations,
unexplained behavioral changes,
recurring patterns that no single participant recognizes.

Taken individually, these reports appear meaningless.

Viewed collectively—and compared against evidence gathered during the investigation—they become compelling corroboration that the Utica disappearances are neither unique nor entirely unprecedented.

The thread therefore rewards disciplined skepticism rather than blanket dismissal.

Investigators who reject it entirely overlook valuable evidence.

Investigators who believe it uncritically become lost in misinformation.

Success depends upon separating signal from noise.

Changes Over Time

The discussion evolves continuously throughout the operation.

As local news coverage expands, participation increases dramatically.

New users arrive daily.

Rumors spread rapidly.

False claims multiply.

If Daniel Kowalski disappears, speculation intensifies almost immediately.

Some users accuse law enforcement.

Others insist the disappearances prove an extraterrestrial invasion.

A few begin organizing amateur expeditions into Hogsback State Forest, creating additional pressure on local authorities and increasing the risk of civilian exposure.

If Brooks remains active, he occasionally intervenes to redirect discussions toward verifiable evidence, though even he struggles to keep speculation under control.

Deleted posts, moderator actions, and account removals ensure the thread remains a constantly changing source rather than a permanent record.

Handler Notes

The SaucerWatch thread exists to illustrate that the internet is neither an oracle nor a cesspool—it is both simultaneously.

Unlike Brooks' archive, which represents disciplined individual investigation, the thread represents collective intelligence operating without central coordination.

Most contributions are inaccurate.

Many are harmful.

Some are intentionally deceptive.

Yet genuine observations occasionally survive precisely because thousands of unrelated people are watching different parts of the world.

The investigators' task is not to decide whether the internet is trustworthy.

It is to determine which observations deserve trust.

This artifact reinforces one of the operation's recurring themes:

Truth does not become less true simply because it appears alongside nonsense.

Likewise, nonsense does not become true simply because thousands of people repeat it.

The investigators' responsibility is to distinguish between the two.
