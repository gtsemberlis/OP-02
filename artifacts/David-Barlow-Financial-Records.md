# A-03 — David Barlow Financial Records

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

Artifact: David Barlow's Financial Records
## Description

A collection of financial records obtained through subpoenas, search warrants, or voluntary production during the FBI investigation.

The records include:

Personal and business bank statements.
Tax returns.
Credit card records.
Payroll documents.
Invoices.
Customer contracts.
Accounting software backups.
Business correspondence.
Loan applications.
Receipts and expense records.

The documents span several years and paint the picture of a moderately successful HVAC contractor whose bookkeeping becomes increasingly irregular over time.

Nothing within the records directly references the disappearances or any other aspect of the current investigation.

## Who Possesses It?

The FBI eventually compiles much of the material as part of its financial review.

David maintains original copies of many business records at his residence and office.

Investigators may gain access through official channels or by searching his property.

## Where Is It Found?

Records are divided between:

David's home office.
His HVAC business.
Banking institutions.
IRS filings.
Financial software stored on his desktop computer.

No single location contains the complete picture.

## Initial Interpretation

The records immediately explain why David became defensive during FBI interviews.

Investigators quickly discover evidence of ongoing financial crimes, including:

Underreported business income.
Cash payments omitted from tax filings.
Inflated business deductions.
Payroll irregularities involving seasonal employees.
Inconsistent accounting practices.

The pattern appears deliberate rather than accidental.

David has legitimate reasons to fear federal investigators.

Importantly, none of these offenses bear any apparent relationship to the disappearances.

## Further Examination
Financial Accounting

A systematic review confirms that David has been committing tax and accounting fraud for several years.

The fraud appears motivated entirely by personal financial gain.

No unusual payments, unexplained transfers, or hidden expenditures connect the activity to the current investigation.

Law

The evidence would likely support multiple state and federal financial charges.

While serious, the offenses are routine.

Investigators familiar with financial crimes immediately recognize them as ordinary white-collar misconduct rather than evidence of organized criminal activity.

Interview Analysis

Understanding David's financial situation significantly reframes his earlier behavior.

His evasiveness, hostility, and reluctance to cooperate no longer appear inherently suspicious.

He believes the FBI has uncovered his financial crimes.

His fear is genuine.

It is simply directed toward the wrong problem.

## Relationships

The records primarily relate to David Barlow himself.

They reinforce:

His defensive behavior during interviews.
Testimony provided by his ex-wife.
His impulsive purchase of a handgun following FBI attention.

The records contain no meaningful connections to:

the Amateur Radio Club,
the disappearances,
SaucerWatch,
the anomalous radio signal,
or the Mi-Go.

That absence becomes the artifact's most important finding.

## Investigation Impact

David's financial records eliminate one of the investigation's most convincing false leads.

Early in the operation, investigators may reasonably suspect that David's nervous behavior, inconsistent statements, and growing paranoia indicate deeper involvement in the disappearances.

The records reveal a far simpler explanation.

David is guilty.

Just not of this.

Recognizing this allows investigators to separate ordinary criminal behavior from the Unnatural.

The records may also influence future interactions with David.

Investigators who discreetly reassure him that they are not pursuing financial crimes may find him substantially more cooperative.

Conversely, investigators willing to exploit his fears can pressure or manipulate him into providing information he might otherwise withhold.

Either approach carries ethical and operational consequences.

## Changes Over Time

The financial records themselves never change.

David's interpretation of the investigators' intentions does.

If he comes to believe they have uncovered his financial misconduct, he becomes increasingly anxious, secretive, and prone to poor decisions.

If investigators successfully convince him that his accounting problems are not their concern, much of his defensiveness disappears, allowing conversations to focus on his disappearance rather than his finances.

## Handler Notes

David Barlow's financial records exist to demonstrate that not every suspicious person is connected to the Unnatural.

Real investigations are filled with unrelated crimes, embarrassing secrets, and innocent explanations for apparently suspicious behavior.

The artifact rewards investigators who pursue evidence rather than assumptions.

David's guilt should never become a "gotcha."

His financial crimes are real.

His fear of the FBI is justified.

His anxiety simply points toward an entirely different investigation.

Perhaps more importantly, the records provide an opportunity for investigators to define their own professionalism. They can reassure David and earn his trust, or they can leverage knowledge of his crimes to manipulate him. Either choice is effective. Neither is presented as the "correct" one. That decision says as much about the investigators as it does about David.
