# A-06 — Brooks Investigation Report

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

Artifact: Brooks' Investigation Report
## Description

Brooks' investigation is not a single report but a working archive assembled over years of independent research.

It consists of:

Bound field notebooks.
Digital notes stored across multiple hard drives and encrypted USB flash drives.
Printed newspaper articles.
FOIA requests and responses.
Witness interview transcripts.
Maps.
Photographs.
Audio recordings.
Radio monitoring logs.
Archived SaucerWatch forum discussions.
Public records collected from dozens of jurisdictions.

The material is exceptionally well organized.

Individual cases cross-reference one another through timelines, geographic comparisons, witness characteristics, environmental observations, and recurring behavioral patterns. Brooks continually annotates older conclusions as new evidence emerges, often correcting his own assumptions when facts no longer support them.

Much of the archive duplicates information investigators have already collected through official channels.

Its true value lies elsewhere.

Interwoven throughout the collection are five historical investigations involving remarkably similar disappearances.

Across several decades and multiple regions of the United States, Brooks has documented incidents involving:

Three to five individuals disappearing within a short period.
Victims returning days or weeks later with little memory of their absence.
Persistent but subtle personality changes after returning.
Communities gradually accepting the changes without explanation.

One or two historical victims never returned.

None of the incidents were ever officially connected.

Throughout the archive Brooks consistently labels these cases as Alien Abductions.

Whether that conclusion is correct remains an open question.

## Who Possesses It?

Evan Brooks.

The archive accompanies him wherever he travels and effectively serves as his mobile office.

He considers it irreplaceable.

Brooks will never willingly surrender the collection.

Investigators who treat him as a fellow investigator rather than an obstacle may earn supervised access to portions of the archive or receive copies of specific case files.

Investigators who search his motel room without permission could copy portions of the collection, although Brooks is meticulous enough to notice missing files or evidence that someone has disturbed his work.

## Where Is It Found?

Following SaucerWatch's arrival, the archive occupies much of Brooks' motel room.

Investigators find:

Open notebooks covering the desk.
Maps taped to the walls.
External hard drives connected to a laptop.
USB drives sorted in labeled cases.
Boxes of printed records.
Index cards connecting cases across decades.

The room resembles a temporary research office rather than the stereotypical conspiracy theorist's lair.

Everything has a place.

Brooks simply knows where that place is.

## Initial Interpretation

At first examination, much of Brooks' work appears surprisingly familiar.

Investigators recognize newspaper articles, witness statements, technical observations, and public records that largely corroborate information they have already uncovered.

The significant difference emerges when investigators begin reading Brooks' historical case files.

Five previous investigations display striking similarities to the current operation.

Each involves small clusters of disappearances followed by unexplained returns.

Victims consistently exhibit:

Missing time.
Incomplete memories.
Lasting personality changes.
Difficulty describing their experience.

The cases occurred in different states, decades apart, with no obvious geographic or demographic pattern.

Brooks believes they represent recurring alien abductions.

The archive clearly demonstrates recurring patterns.

Whether those patterns represent extraterrestrial activity remains entirely speculative.

## Further Examination
Investigative Analysis

A careful review reveals Brooks to be an exceptionally disciplined researcher.

He consistently distinguishes:

verified fact,
witness testimony,
inference,
speculation.

When new evidence contradicts earlier conclusions, Brooks revises his notes rather than forcing the evidence to fit his beliefs.

Investigators may disagree with his conclusions.

They cannot honestly dismiss the quality of his research.

History

Several historical cases can be independently verified through archived newspapers, missing person reports, and local government records.

Brooks did not invent these incidents.

He recognized similarities that no official agency appears to have connected.

Intelligence / Federal Law Enforcement

None of the historical disappearances appear to have generated sustained federal attention.

Cases remained isolated within local jurisdictions before gradually fading from public memory.

This naturally raises an uncomfortable operational question:

Has the Program encountered these cases before?

Psychology

Across multiple decades, returned victims consistently demonstrate similar behavioral changes.

Many become:

calmer,
more patient,
less anxious,
emotionally resilient,
unusually accepting of previous hardships.

These changes appear authentic rather than performative.

Computer Science

Metadata associated with Brooks' digital archive confirms that much of the research predates the Utica disappearances by many years.

This is not a theory assembled after the current investigation began.

It is an evolving body of research.

Anthropology / Sociology

Brooks' archive demonstrates that communities consistently explain extraordinary experiences using the cultural framework available to them.

Depending on the era, witnesses describe:

flying saucers,
government conspiracies,
secret military projects,
angels,
demons,
psychological trauma.

The reported experiences remain remarkably consistent.

Only the explanations change.

## Relationships

Brooks' archive gradually becomes connected to nearly every major component of the investigation.

It references:

Hal Whitaker's radio observations.
Nate Collins' contact with SaucerWatch.
Samantha Carter's disappearance.
Daniel Kowalski's search.
Amateur Radio Club activity.
SaucerWatch discussions.
Public reporting.
Historical newspaper archives.

As Brooks continues investigating, his report increasingly mirrors the investigators' own case file, approaching many of the same conclusions through entirely different methods.

## Investigation Impact

Brooks' archive changes the investigation in three important ways.

Comparative Cases

Until this point, investigators may reasonably believe they are confronting a unique event.

Brooks demonstrates that remarkably similar incidents have occurred repeatedly across the United States.

Whether or not his explanation is correct, the pattern itself appears genuine.

For Delta Green investigators, this naturally suggests the possibility of undisclosed institutional knowledge within the Program.

The archive encourages comparative thinking without transforming the operation into a historical investigation.

Alternative Interview Framework

Brooks' greatest contribution is not his conclusions.

It is the questions he asks.

Having spent years interviewing alleged alien abduction victims, Brooks has developed an interview style fundamentally different from conventional law enforcement.

Rather than focusing on chronology or factual reconstruction, Brooks patiently explores fragmented sensory memories.

His interviews emphasize:

Physical sensations.
Sounds.
Temperature.
Terrain.
Changes in elevation.
Spatial awareness.
Missing time.
Fleeting impressions that witnesses themselves often dismiss.

He is unusually comfortable allowing silence, uncertainty, and incomplete memories to remain unresolved.

Ironically, this often produces more useful information than attempts to force witnesses into coherent narratives.

New Witness Testimony

Because Brooks asks different questions, he recovers different answers.

Nathaniel Collins, initially skeptical of Brooks' extraterrestrial conclusions, nevertheless agrees to compare observations with another technically minded investigator. Brooks' patient questioning eventually recovers several fragmented memories Nate never considered important enough to mention during official interviews.

Nate recalls:

Walking for an indeterminate period.
The terrain becoming progressively steeper.
Feeling unusually tired despite little memory of exertion.
Hearing moving water.
Emerging briefly into an area that felt unexpectedly open.
The anomalous radio buzzing becoming noticeably sharper rather than simply louder.

None of these memories identify a location.

Together, however, they strongly suggest a high-elevation overlook somewhere near flowing water.

Christopher Donnelly provides an entirely different category of observations after lengthy conversations with Brooks. Christopher never describes extraordinary beings or impossible technology. Instead, he recalls details only a precision machinist would notice.

Among his fragmented memories:

"The floor didn't feel like rock."

After reflection:

"Not concrete either."

Another pause.

"More like... something machined."

Later he remembers another peculiar detail.

"Nothing rattled."

Pressed for clarification, Christopher explains that every machine vibrates.

Whatever environment he briefly occupied did not.

These observations do not explain what happened.

They do suggest that the environment was intentionally constructed rather than naturally occurring.

Combined with the investigators' own technical evidence, these recovered memories become valuable navigational clues rather than supernatural revelations.

Brooks believes they support alien abduction.

The investigators recognize them as objective observations awaiting explanation.

## Changes Over Time

Brooks' archive continues evolving throughout the operation.

Every day he remains active, new interviews, maps, timelines, and comparisons are added.

If Daniel Kowalski disappears, Brooks immediately begins integrating the new disappearance into his historical comparisons.

If investigators cooperate with Brooks, he willingly incorporates their observations while carefully distinguishing firsthand evidence from secondhand information.

His archive gradually becomes an unofficial counterpart to the investigators' own case file.

If investigators instead treat Brooks as an adversary, his behavior changes.

Sensitive files disappear.

Encrypted backups appear.

Critical documents are relocated to secure off-site storage.

Brooks does not become hostile.

He simply assumes his work may need to survive without him.

## Handler Notes

Brooks' Investigation Report exists to demonstrate that asking different questions often reveals different evidence.

Brooks is neither a crank nor a secret expert on the Unnatural.

He is a capable civilian investigator interpreting genuine observations through the only explanatory framework available to him.

His greatest strength is not his theory.

It is his willingness to let witnesses describe impossible experiences without immediately rejecting, correcting, or explaining them.

That approach recovers memories that conventional interviews leave undiscovered.

The investigators should ultimately reject Brooks' explanation while recognizing the value of his methodology.

His archive therefore serves as both a comparative case library and a reminder that investigative success often depends less on having better answers than on asking better questions.
