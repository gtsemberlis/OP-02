# A-10 — Mi-Go Artifacts

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

rtifact: Mi-Go Artifacts
## Description

Scattered throughout the Mi-Go operating chamber are dozens of objects that investigators immediately recognize as manufactured.

Some resemble tools.

Others appear to be instruments, storage containers, measuring devices, or components of larger systems.

None resemble any known human technology.

Recovered objects vary considerably in size and form.

Examples include:

Metallic lattice structures with no obvious moving parts.
Transparent crystalline rods containing slowly shifting internal patterns.
Flexible black sheets that are neither fabric nor metal.
Hollow geometric frames that subtly change shape when viewed from different angles.
Smooth spherical objects that emit faint vibrations when handled.
Delicate filament bundles connected to unfamiliar biological materials.
Dense alloy blocks whose apparent weight changes depending upon how they are held.

No markings, language, labels, or symbols identify their purpose.

The collection appears less like a cache of exotic treasures than the ordinary equipment of an active workplace.

## Who Possesses It?

The Mi-Go.

Most artifacts remain within the operating chamber.

Investigators may recover individual objects if circumstances permit, though doing so risks delaying escape, contaminating evidence, or attracting unwanted attention.

Recovered objects become Program property following the operation.

## Where Is It Found?

Artifacts are distributed naturally throughout the operating chamber.

Some rest on work surfaces.

Others remain integrated into larger assemblies.

Several appear abandoned temporarily rather than deliberately stored.

Nothing suggests the Mi-Go consider these objects especially valuable.

## Initial Interpretation

Investigators immediately recognize that every object is artificial.

Equally apparent is the complete inability to determine what any individual object actually does.

Nothing resembles recognizable human technology closely enough to support meaningful analogies.

The objects feel purposeful.

Just not interpretable.

## Further Examination
Materials Science

The recovered materials demonstrate combinations of strength, flexibility, density, and thermal behavior inconsistent with known terrestrial manufacturing.

Several objects appear to incorporate both biological and non-biological components seamlessly.

No investigator can confidently identify where one ends and the other begins.

Engineering

Every object appears exceptionally well engineered.

No exposed fasteners.

No obvious access panels.

No conventional mechanical interfaces.

Many objects possess geometries that seem optimized for purposes entirely unrelated to human ergonomics.

Investigators quickly conclude that they cannot infer function from appearance.

Physics

Several artifacts display subtle physical properties that resist conventional explanation.

Examples may include:

anomalous thermal behavior,
unusual resonance,
unexpected inertial effects,
optical characteristics inconsistent with their apparent structure.

None violate known physical laws outright.

They simply operate in ways investigators cannot adequately explain.

Biology

Some objects appear partially organic.

Others appear entirely synthetic.

Many defy the distinction altogether.

The Mi-Go evidently make little conceptual separation between living systems and manufactured devices.

Comparative Analysis

No two recovered objects appear intended for the same task.

Rather than suggesting a unified technological system, the collection resembles the miscellaneous tools one might expect to find scattered throughout any functioning laboratory or workshop.

This realization is perhaps the most unsettling observation of all.

If these are merely ordinary tools...

what would extraordinary Mi-Go technology look like?

## Relationships

The artifacts reinforce the broader understanding established by the operating chamber.

They support no specific investigative lead.

Instead, they contextualize everything investigators have already learned.

The objects demonstrate that:

the chamber is actively maintained,
the Mi-Go conduct ongoing work,
the neurological equipment is only one part of a much larger technological ecosystem.

Recovered artifacts may later become Program research subjects, though no immediate operational value is apparent.

## Investigation Impact

Unlike most artifacts in the operation, Mi-Go Artifacts answer remarkably few questions.

Instead, they generate new ones.

Investigators leave the chamber understanding only one thing with certainty:

Human technological assumptions no longer apply.

Attempts to classify recovered objects by familiar categories—weapon, computer, medical device, tool—rapidly fail.

The artifacts resist interpretation.

Their greatest investigative value lies not in revealing Mi-Go capabilities, but in demonstrating the profound limits of human understanding.

For Delta Green, however, even apparently useless recovery has immense value.

Every recovered object represents an opportunity for future comparative analysis.

Today's incomprehensible artifact may become tomorrow's point of recognition in another operation.

## Changes Over Time

The artifacts themselves do not change.

Their interpretation does.

Objects initially dismissed as meaningless curiosities may later resemble equipment encountered in future investigations.

Conversely, attempts at immediate classification often prove increasingly inadequate as investigators reflect upon what they observed inside the chamber.

The Program archives recovered artifacts carefully.

Not because they understand them.

Because someday they might.

## Handler Notes

Mi-Go Artifacts exist to reinforce one of the operation's most important philosophical principles:

Taxonomy should organize ignorance. Not eliminate it.

The investigators should never leave the chamber believing they have acquired alien technology.

They have acquired alien objects.

Those are not the same thing.

Every instinct encourages players to ask:

"What does this do?"

The correct answer is almost always:

"We don't know."

That uncertainty is not failure.

It is honest investigation.

The Program does not collect artifacts because it expects immediate understanding.

It collects them because comparison across decades of operations occasionally reveals patterns invisible within any single case.

The recovered objects therefore become part of the Program's institutional memory.

Not solutions.

Not weapons.

Not rewards.

Evidence.
