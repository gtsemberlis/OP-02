# HT-02.10 — Interview Guide — Richard “Rick” Holloway

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## Default Timing

This guide assumes investigators interview Rick during Act I (June 27–July 1), after reviewing the FBI case file and identifying the Mohawk Valley Amateur Radio Club as the strongest connection among the victims. Rick has known every victim personally for years and willingly cooperates with investigators. He knows nothing about the Unnatural and views the disappearances as an incomprehensible tragedy affecting a close-knit community.

## General Guidance

Rick is warm, welcoming, and immediately puts investigators at ease.

Unlike most witnesses, he doesn't begin by asking why they're there.

He asks if they would like coffee.

Rick naturally talks about people rather than events. He rarely begins with dates or facts. Instead, he introduces each victim the way someone would introduce an old friend.

If investigators allow him to speak naturally, they gain something far more valuable than isolated facts—they begin to understand the social fabric that connected the victims.

## Interview
"Can you tell us about the club?"

Rick smiles.

"It's a hobby."

"People hear 'amateur radio' and imagine secret codes or conspiracy theories."

He chuckles.

"Mostly it's retirees arguing about antennas over bad coffee."

"How would you describe the membership?"

"Curious."

"Helpful."

"A little eccentric."

"Exactly the sort of people you'd want around when something breaks."

"Were the missing members particularly close?"

He thinks for a moment.

"Some were."

"Some weren't."

"That's normal."

"We weren't one big friend group."

"We were a community."

"Can you tell us about Christopher Donnelly?"

"Chris fixed things."

Immediate answer.

"If a coax connector failed..."

"Chris had already repaired it before anyone noticed."

"Harold Whitaker?"

Rick smiles.

"Professor."

"Not officially."

"That's just what everybody called him."

"You couldn't ask Hal a question without learning something."

"David Barlow?"

Rick laughs.

"First one to volunteer."

"Last one to leave."

"If you needed a tower moved..."

"Dave showed up with a truck before you finished asking."

"Samantha Carter?"

His expression softens.

"Best questions in the room."

"She wasn't interested in sounding smart."

"She genuinely wanted to understand."

"Nathaniel Collins?"

"Quiet."

"Until somebody had a technical problem."

"Then suddenly Nate appeared beside them."

He smiles.

"Fixed it."

"Disappeared again."

"Did these five spend unusual amounts of time together?"

He shakes his head.

"No."

"Not more than anyone else."

"The club mixes generations naturally."

"That's one of the reasons I love it."

"Did they ever work on secret projects together?"

Rick laughs.

"No."

"If we'd been building secret projects..."

"Trust me..."

"Someone would've put it in the newsletter."

"Did you notice anything unusual before the disappearances?"

He grows quiet.

"Not unusual."

"Just..."

A pause.

"Different."

"Different how?"

"People seemed..."

He searches for the word.

"Lighter."

"Less worried."

"I thought everyone was finally getting back to normal after a hard few years."

"When did you realize something was wrong?"

He looks toward the empty chairs around the meeting room.

"Attendance."

"Attendance?"

He nods.

"First Chris missed a meeting."

"Then Hal."

"Then Dave."

"Then Samantha."

"Then Nate."

A long silence.

"Five empty chairs."

"Did you tell anyone?"

"No."

He smiles sadly.

"At first I thought I was imagining a pattern."

"Why didn't you say anything later?"

"Because by then everyone already knew they were missing."

Another pause.

"The chairs just noticed first."

"Do you think someone targeted the club?"

Rick thinks carefully.

"I don't know."

"I hope not."

"I'd rather believe this was random."

He looks around the room.

"Because the alternative means someone chose these people."

"Have the members changed since returning?"

"Yes."

Without hesitation.

"How?"

"Nothing dramatic."

"Chris smiles more."

"Dave doesn't snap at people anymore."

"Hal laughs."

He smiles.

"I hadn't heard that laugh in years."

"That sounds like good news."

Rick nods slowly.

"It does."

Then he frowns.

"That's what's bothering everyone."

"Can you explain?"

"Communities notice change."

"Not just bad change."

"Any change."

"Everyone keeps saying..."

"Doesn't Dave seem calmer?"

"Hasn't Hal been sleeping better?"

"Samantha seems happier."

He sighs.

"Eventually you start wondering why everyone's saying the same thing."

"Do you think they're the same people?"

He answers more slowly than any previous question.

"Mostly."

Another pause.

"Enough that their families recognize them."

He folds his hands.

"Not enough that nobody notices."

"What worries you the most?"

He looks around the meeting room again.

"That everyone keeps treating this like five separate cases."

The investigators wait.

"It wasn't five disappearances."

"It was one community losing five people."

"Is there anything else we should know?"

Rick smiles sadly.

"You'll find evidence."

"Phone records."

"Financial records."

"Radio logs."

He nods toward the meeting hall.

"Just don't forget these people existed before they became evidence."

Another pause.

"The case file begins with five missing persons."

"My story begins with five neighbors."

## Handler Notes

Rick is the operation's humanizing witness. He contributes relatively few critical clues, but he provides something no other NPC can: a coherent picture of the community that existed before the investigation began. By describing each victim through the role they played in the club rather than the circumstances of their disappearance, Rick quietly restores their identities as neighbors, mentors, volunteers, and friends.

The emotional centerpiece of the interview is the image of the five empty chairs. Rick doesn't discover the pattern through forensic analysis or investigative brilliance. He notices it because he knows who normally sits in each seat. That simple observation transforms the disappearances from an abstract investigative problem into a lived communal loss.

His final reminder—that the investigators' files begin with missing persons while his story begins with neighbors—serves as an appropriate conclusion to the entire interview guide. Throughout HT-02, investigators have learned facts, memories, and observations from individual witnesses. Rick reminds them that all of those individual stories belonged to a single community long before they became a Delta Green operation.
