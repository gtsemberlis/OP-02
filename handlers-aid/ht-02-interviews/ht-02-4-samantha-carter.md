# HT-02.4 — Interview Guide — Samantha Carter

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## General Guidance

Sam is literal.

She answers exactly the question asked.

She rarely volunteers additional information unless investigators specifically ask for it.

Long pauses are normal.

She is thinking—not hiding anything.

Investigators who remain patient generally receive fuller answers.

## Interview
"What do you remember before you went missing?"

"Saturday."

"I packed my backpack."

"Camera."

"Water."

"Radio."

"I wanted to test an antenna I'd been working on."

...

"Nothing unusual before I left."

"Everything after that gets... blurry."

"Where were you headed?"

"Somewhere quiet."

"I wanted to test an antenna."

"Take photographs."

She thinks.

"I thought I knew where I was going."

Another pause.

"Now I don't know if I ever got there."

"What was the last thing you clearly remember?"

She thinks for several seconds.

"Walking."

"Taking pictures."

"Watching signal strength."

"Trees."

"That's all in the right order."

She looks uncertain.

"I just can't attach it to a place."

"Do you remember seeing anyone?"

"No."

"Not people."

Another pause.

"I don't remember being alone either."

"Do you remember any sounds?"

"The radio."

"Wind."

"Birds."

She hesitates.

"Maybe."

"I don't know if that's a memory."

"Any smells?"

"Pine."

"Wet leaves."

"Nothing unusual."

"Temperature?"

"Cool."

"Not cold."

"Comfortable."

"Did you ever feel like you were in danger?"

She answers immediately.

"No."

"What do you remember next?"

"Opening my eyes."

"My backpack was beside me."

"Everything was wet."

She thinks.

"Not raining."

"Just..."

"Wet."

"Were you injured?"

"No."

"Hungry."

"Mostly confused."

"Have you been feeling different since you came back?"

This answer takes noticeably longer.

"Maybe."

She isn't evasive.

She's choosing her words carefully.

"People..."

A pause.

"People used to feel loud."

"Now they don't."

She looks genuinely puzzled.

"I know that doesn't make sense."

"What do you mean by 'loud?'"

"Not actual noise."

"Just..."

She searches for the word.

"Processing."

"Talking to people used to take more work."

"Now it doesn't."

Another pause.

"It's easier."

"Does that bother you?"

Another long pause.

"Yes."

The investigators may expect her to explain why.

She doesn't.

Eventually:

"Because I don't know why."

"Has anyone else noticed?"

"My parents."

"Hal."

"Nate would have."

She smiles slightly.

"He notices things."

Then the smile fades.

"I guess he still does."

"Do you think whatever happened to you was good?"

The longest silence yet.

"That's the wrong question."

The investigators wait.

"Something happened."

"Some parts of it seem good."

"That doesn't mean it should have happened."

This is probably the longest answer she's given during the interview.

"Is there anything else you remember?"

She thinks.

Longer than before.

Finally—

"I keep feeling like I forgot something important."

"Not because I can't remember it."

Another pause.

"Because I don't think I ever understood it."

## Handler Notes

Sam should never sound frightened or melodramatic.

She is approaching the experience exactly as she approaches an engineering problem: carefully, honestly, and without embellishment. She does not speculate, fill gaps with guesses, or exaggerate uncertainty. When she says "I don't know," she genuinely means it.

One subtle but powerful cue is the contrast between her communication before and after the disappearance. Her speech remains literal and deliberate, but eye contact comes more naturally, pauses feel less strained, and social interaction appears less mentally taxing. These changes are modest enough that an investigator might initially interpret them as growing confidence—until multiple interviews reveal that her parents, mentors, and friends all noticed the same shift.
