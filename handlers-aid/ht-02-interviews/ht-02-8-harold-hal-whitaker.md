# HT-02.8 — Interview Guide — Harold “Hal” Whitaker

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## Default Timing

This guide assumes investigators interview Harold during Act I (June 27–July 1), after his return but before later events draw him back toward the investigation. Harold has already spent considerable time examining his own experience with the same disciplined skepticism he once applied to engineering problems. He knows nothing of the Unnatural, but he has become deeply uncomfortable with the unexplained changes he observes in himself.

## General Guidance

Hal is patient, thoughtful, and never rushed.

He often pauses before answering—not because he is hiding information, but because he believes accurate answers deserve careful thought.

He dislikes speculation presented as certainty.

If investigators admit uncertainty, Hal respects them immediately.

If they jump to conclusions, he gently challenges their reasoning rather than arguing with them.

## Interview
"Can you tell us what happened?"

He smiles faintly.

"If I could..."

A pause.

"You probably wouldn't be here."

"What do you remember about that day?"

"Packing my radio."

"Checking the weather."

"Driving."

"Walking."

"Monitoring signal strength."

He thinks.

"After that..."

He folds his hands together.

"Nothing reliable."

"Where were you going?"

"Somewhere quiet."

"Somewhere I could test an antenna."

He smiles.

"At seventy-one, one wooded hill starts looking much like another."

Another pause.

"I don't remember where I ultimately chose to go."

"Do you remember hearing anything unusual?"

"Interference."

"Persistent."

"Broadband."

He nods slightly.

"Interesting enough that I intended to investigate it."

"Did you ever determine what caused it?"

"No."

Without hesitation.

"If I had..."

A faint smile.

"You wouldn't be asking."

"Do you remember seeing anyone?"

"No."

He thinks.

"Or rather..."

"I don't possess a memory I would trust enough to answer yes."

"What do you remember next?"

"Opening my eyes."

"Sitting on damp ground."

"Radio beside me."

"Watch still running."

He taps the analog watch on his wrist.

"Everything else was..."

He searches for the word.

"Absent."

"Did you think you had been injured?"

"Initially."

"That seemed the most likely explanation."

"Unfortunately..."

A slight smile.

"The evidence didn't cooperate."

"Have you noticed any changes since returning?"

"Yes."

Immediately.

"What kind of changes?"

"I sleep."

He lets the words hang in the air.

"All night."

"Is that unusual?"

He nods.

"For six years."

"Since Margaret died."

A long silence.

"I had forgotten what uninterrupted sleep felt like."

"Has anything else changed?"

"My knees hurt less."

"My appetite has returned."

"I laugh more."

He smiles faintly.

"Apparently."

"That all sounds positive."

"It does."

He agrees.

"Which is precisely the problem."

"Why is that a problem?"

He leans back slightly.

"Engineering teaches a simple lesson."

"Every observable effect has a cause."

"If the effect is beneficial..."

"That does not eliminate the need to understand the cause."

"You don't think you've simply healed?"

He shakes his head.

"Healing has a history."

"Recovery leaves footprints."

"This..."

A pause.

"Feels discontinuous."

"Can you explain that?"

"Grief changes slowly."

"Day by day."

"Conversation by conversation."

"Memory by memory."

He folds his hands again.

"Mine appears to have..."

Another pause.

"Stepped aside."

"Do you miss it?"

The question surprises him.

He thinks for a long time.

"Yes."

"You miss your grief?"

"Of course."

He says it gently.

"It belonged to Margaret."

"It belonged to loving her."

"If something removed it..."

He looks down.

"Then something removed part of my relationship with her."

"Do you think you're still the same man?"

He smiles sadly.

"I don't know."

"I hope so."

"Hope is not evidence."

"Have you been trying to figure it out?"

He nods.

"Naturally."

"How?"

"Observation."

"Documentation."

"Comparison."

"The same way I'd investigate any unexpected phenomenon."

"And what have you concluded?"

"Very little."

A faint smile.

"Good investigations often begin that way."

"What do you think happened to you?"

He is silent for several seconds.

"I don't know."

"I know something happened."

"I know I cannot explain it."

"Everything beyond that would be speculation."

"Does that uncertainty bother you?"

"Less than it used to."

He smiles.

"Age has certain advantages."

"You eventually become comfortable admitting ignorance."

"Then what worries you?"

He answers quietly.

"The temptation to accept a pleasant outcome without asking how it was achieved."

Another pause.

"History is filled with intelligent people who ignored uncomfortable questions because they liked the answers."

"Is there anything else we should know?"

He looks toward the shelves filled with engineering notebooks.

"If you find something that contradicts what I've told you..."

He smiles politely.

"Believe the evidence."

"Not me."

The investigators wait.

"Memory is only one instrument."

"Good engineering never depends upon a single instrument."

## Handler Notes

Hal is not the operation's smartest witness because he knows the most—he is its smartest witness because he understands the limits of what he knows. Every answer reflects decades of engineering discipline: separate observation from explanation, distrust conclusions that arrive too easily, and never mistake a beneficial outcome for proof that the underlying process was benign.

The emotional centerpiece of the interview is his discussion of grief. Hal is not frightened because he feels better; he is frightened because grief was an expression of his love for Margaret. If that grief has vanished without the slow work of healing, then something has altered a deeply human part of him. Unlike the other victims, Hal recognizes that the loss of suffering may itself be a loss.

His final advice—"Believe the evidence. Not me."—should linger with the investigators. It captures both Hal's character and one of the operation's defining investigative principles: witnesses, even honest ones, are only one source of evidence. Truth emerges through disciplined comparison, not unquestioning belief.
