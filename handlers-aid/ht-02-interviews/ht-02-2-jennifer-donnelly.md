# HT-02.2 — Interview Guide — Jennifer Donnelly

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## Default Timing

This guide assumes investigators interview Jennifer during Act I (June 27–July 1), shortly after Christopher's return. She has had only a few days to process his disappearance and reappearance. She knows nothing about the Unnatural and only limited information about the other disappearances.

## General Guidance

Jennifer is composed, practical, and accustomed to remaining calm during difficult conversations.

She is not suspicious of investigators and genuinely wants to help. Years of nursing have taught her to answer questions methodically and without embellishment. When she doesn't know something, she says so plainly.

Unlike Chris, she answers quickly. Only questions about the emotional impact of the disappearance cause her to pause.

## Interview
"Can you tell me about Chris?"

She smiles immediately.

"He's dependable."

"Quiet."

"If something in the house breaks, he disappears into the garage for an hour and comes back with it fixed."

A small laugh.

"Sometimes improved."

"What was he like before he disappeared?"

"Pretty much the same man I've known for seventeen years."

"Work."

"Kids."

"His workshop."

"Radio club once or twice a month."

"Not very exciting."

She smiles.

"That's one of the reasons I married him."

"How was your marriage?"

"Good."

She answers without hesitation.

"Still is."

"We argue about normal things."

"Money."

"Whose turn it is to drive the kids."

"Nothing unusual."

"Did he seem worried about anything?"

She thinks.

"The kids growing up."

"Mostly."

"He'd never admit it, but he worried about not spending enough time with them."

"That's about it."

"Did anything seem unusual before he disappeared?"

"No."

Then she hesitates.

"Not unusual."

"Different."

"Different how?"

"He seemed..."

She searches for the word.

"Lighter."

"Less tired."

"More present."

"He laughed more."

"When did you first notice that?"

"A few weeks before he disappeared."

"Maybe a month."

She shakes her head.

"I didn't write it down."

"I just remember thinking..."

A faint smile.

"Whatever was bothering him finally wasn't."

"Did he ever tell you why?"

"No."

"Honestly..."

She smiles.

"I didn't ask."

"I thought I'd gotten lucky."

"Can you tell us about the day he disappeared?"

"It was completely ordinary."

"He had breakfast."

"Reminded Erin to finish her science project."

"Kissed me goodbye."

"Loaded the truck."

"Left."

She looks down.

"That's it."

"Did he tell you where he was going?"

"Out with the radio."

"Maybe hiking."

She shrugs.

"He had a handful of places he liked."

"I never kept track of which one."

"When did you realize something was wrong?"

"Dinner."

"Chris is almost never late."

"If he was going to be late, he'd call."

"He didn't."

"What did you do?"

"Called his phone."

"Called a couple people from the radio club."

"Then the Sheriff's Office."

She says it matter-of-factly.

"After that..."

"Everything became paperwork."

"Did you ever think he had left voluntarily?"

She looks genuinely surprised.

"No."

Immediately.

"Absolutely not."

"Why are you so certain?"

"Because I know my husband."

A pause.

"If Chris wanted to leave..."

"He'd tell me."

"Has he changed since he came home?"

She smiles.

"Yes."

Then the smile slowly fades.

"That's the part I don't know what to do with."

"What changed?"

"He quit smoking."

"Overnight."

"Twenty years."

"Just..."

She snaps her fingers.

"Done."

"Anything else?"

"He's sleeping."

"Really sleeping."

"He used to wake up three or four times a night."

"Now he sleeps straight through."

"How about with the children?"

Her expression softens.

"He's more... present."

"He listens."

"He doesn't drift off thinking about work anymore."

"He's always been a good father."

"Now..."

She smiles sadly.

"He's easier to reach."

"Does that make you happy?"

She doesn't answer immediately.

"Yes."

A pause.

"It also scares me."

"Why?"

"Because people don't usually become better versions of themselves overnight."

Another pause.

"Not without a reason."

"Do you think something happened to him?"

She looks uncomfortable.

"Something happened."

"I don't know what."

"I just know..."

She glances toward the hallway where Chris would likely be.

"He came home."

"But he didn't come home exactly the same."

"Is there anything you haven't told anyone?"

She thinks for a long time.

"I feel guilty for missing him while he was gone."

The investigators wait.

"And I feel guilty for being grateful for some of the changes after he came back."

Another pause.

"I don't know how to hold both of those feelings at the same time."

"What worries you the most?"

She answers quietly.

"That one day he'll tell me he remembers."

"And whatever he remembers..."

She shakes her head.

"Will explain why he isn't quite the same man anymore."

## Handler Notes

Jennifer's interview should never feel like a clue dump. She is one of the operation's emotional anchors, grounding the investigation in the ordinary rhythms of family life. Her value lies in establishing a stable baseline for Christopher's personality and making the post-return changes feel both unmistakably real and deeply ambiguous.

Most importantly, Jennifer reframes the changes not as symptoms or miracles, but as a source of moral discomfort. She loves many of the differences she sees in her husband—his calmness, his engagement with their children, his freedom from smoking—and that is precisely what frightens her. The horror is not that Christopher returned broken; it is that he returned improved in ways no one can explain. That quiet contradiction echoes through the families of the other victims and becomes one of the operation's defining themes.
