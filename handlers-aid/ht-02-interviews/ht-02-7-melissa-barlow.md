# HT-02.7 — Interview Guide — Melissa Barlow

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## Default Timing

This guide assumes investigators interview Melissa during Act I (June 27–July 1), shortly after Dave's return. Melissa has remained in regular contact with Dave despite their divorce and has observed both his struggles before the disappearance and the subtle changes afterward. She knows nothing about the Unnatural and understands Dave's financial problems only from years of firsthand experience.

## General Guidance

Melissa is calm, direct, and emotionally honest.

She has spent years separating Dave's mistakes from Dave himself. She answers difficult questions without bitterness or defensiveness, and she never exaggerates to make herself look better.

Unlike Dave, she isn't afraid of investigators.

Unlike Jennifer, she isn't trying to preserve an image of her spouse.

She has already accepted that people can love someone who continually disappoints them.

## Interview
"Can you tell us about Dave?"

A small smile.

"He's one of the kindest stubborn men you'll ever meet."

She laughs quietly.

"Those two things explain most of his life."

"What do you mean?"

"He'd drive an hour at two in the morning because an elderly customer lost heat."

"Then he'd ignore his own taxes for six months."

She shrugs.

"That was Dave."

"Why did your marriage end?"

She doesn't hesitate.

"Exhaustion."

"Not anger."

"Not infidelity."

"Just..."

She searches for the word.

"Promises."

"Promises?"

"Every year was going to be the year everything got caught up."

"Next season."

"Next contract."

"Next busy winter."

A sad smile.

"Eventually you realize next year isn't a plan."

"Did you stop loving him?"

She shakes her head immediately.

"No."

"I stopped believing he could change."

"What was he like before he disappeared?"

"Scared."

The answer comes quickly.

"He tried very hard to pretend he wasn't."

"He wasn't fooling anyone who knew him."

"Scared of what?"

"Money."

"The business."

"The books."

She sighs.

"Consequences."

"Did he ever tell you exactly what was wrong?"

"Not exactly."

"Dave hated saying things out loud once they became real."

"Did you notice anything changing before he disappeared?"

She smiles.

Then the smile fades almost immediately.

"Yes."

"What changed?"

"He started apologizing."

A pause.

"Real apologies."

"Not..."

She waves one hand.

"I'm sorry you got upset."

"Actual apologies."

"For what?"

"Everything."

"Missed birthdays."

"Broken promises."

"Being absent."

"Things that happened ten years ago."

"How did you react?"

She laughs softly.

"I thought he was having a midlife crisis."

Another pause.

"Turns out..."

She shrugs.

"I have no idea what it was."

"Did you believe him?"

She thinks carefully.

"Yes."

"For the first time in years."

"Why?"

"Because he wasn't apologizing to make me forgive him."

"He was apologizing because he finally understood why it mattered."

"Did he seem happier?"

"Peaceful."

She corrects herself almost automatically.

"That's the better word."

"How so?"

"He stopped acting like every conversation was another problem he had to solve."

"He could just..."

She smiles faintly.

"Be there."

"Has he changed since he came home?"

She nods.

"More of the same."

"Calmer."

"Kinder."

"More honest."

A pause.

"Which is why none of this makes any sense."

"Why doesn't it make sense?"

"People don't become their best selves because they disappeared for a week."

"Life doesn't work that way."

"Do you think he's hiding something?"

She thinks.

"About the finances?"

"Probably was."

"About the disappearance?"

She shakes her head.

"No."

"Dave couldn't fake confusion if he tried."

"We understand he recently bought a revolver."

For the first time, she looks genuinely surprised.

"He did?"

A long pause.

"That..."

She exhales slowly.

"That scares me."

"Why?"

"Because Dave never wanted a gun."

"Ever."

She thinks for a moment.

Then quietly:

"He wasn't buying a weapon."

Another pause.

"He was buying the feeling that maybe everything wasn't falling apart."

"Do you think he was dangerous?"

She answers immediately.

"No."

"Dangerous?"

She almost laughs.

"Dave cries during dog food commercials."

The smile disappears.

"He's dangerous to himself when he's ashamed."

"What worries you the most?"

She doesn't answer immediately.

"I'm afraid..."

Long pause.

"I'm afraid I got exactly what I'd wanted for twenty years."

The investigators wait.

"I wanted Dave to become this man."

She looks down.

"I just never imagined it would happen like this."

"If you could ask him one question, what would it be?"

She smiles sadly.

"I'd ask if he feels like himself."

Another pause.

"Because from the outside..."

"He finally does."

"Is there anything else we should know?"

She thinks for a long time.

"Don't let his mistakes become the whole story."

"Dave lied about money."

"He avoided problems."

"He hurt people."

She nods once.

"All of that's true."

Then she looks directly at the investigators.

"It's also true that he spent twenty years trying to become a better man."

A long silence.

"Whatever happened to him..."

"It seems to have finished the job."

She immediately looks uncomfortable with her own words.

"And I don't know whether that's beautiful..."

Another pause.

"Or terrifying."

## Handler Notes

Melissa's interview should resist the temptation to turn Dave into either a sympathetic victim or a cautionary tale. She is uniquely positioned to speak about the gap between the man Dave was and the man he wanted to become, and she does so without sentimentality. Her observations carry weight because they come from someone who loved him enough to leave him.

The emotional core of this interview is not Dave's financial misconduct—it is Melissa's dawning realization that the qualities she spent years hoping he would develop have appeared almost overnight. She cannot deny that the changes are real, and she cannot deny that they are, in many ways, good. That leaves her trapped between gratitude and dread.

By the end of the conversation, investigators should understand that the operation's central question is no longer simply "What happened to these people?" It has become something much more unsettling:

"If an outside force removes the parts of us that cause suffering—fear, shame, anxiety, anger—what remains is often a better person. But is it still the same person?"
