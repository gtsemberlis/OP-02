# HT-02.9 — Interview Guide — Rebecca Lawson

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## Default Timing

This guide assumes investigators interview Rebecca during Act I (June 27–July 1), shortly after Harold's return. Rebecca has spent years caring for her widowed father and has observed both his long grief after Margaret's death and the profound changes that preceded and followed his disappearance. She knows nothing about the Unnatural and is trying to reconcile what she has witnessed with everything she understands about grief, healing, and her father.

## General Guidance

Rebecca is composed, articulate, and scientifically minded.

She approaches difficult conversations the same way she approaches her classroom: patiently, methodically, and with genuine curiosity. She respects investigators who distinguish evidence from speculation and who are willing to admit uncertainty.

She genuinely wants answers.

She also believes investigations have ethical obligations.

If investigators become accusatory, repeatedly imply that Hal is mentally impaired, or continue asking the same questions without developing new evidence, Rebecca begins to suspect they are no longer conducting an investigation—they are simply looking for someone to blame.

She remains courteous throughout.

But her trust rapidly disappears.

If she concludes her father is being unfairly scrutinized, she later contacts Chief Patrick O'Connor to express her concerns and ask that local authorities ensure Hal is not subjected to unnecessary harassment. This does not prevent the investigation from continuing, but it changes the social landscape and reinforces that interviews have consequences.

## Interview
"Can you tell us about your father?"

She smiles immediately.

"He's the smartest person I've ever known."

A pause.

"He'd hate hearing me say that."

A small laugh.

"He'd spend ten minutes listing people who were smarter."

"What kind of man is he?"

"Patient."

"Curious."

"Kind."

"He never made anyone feel foolish for asking questions."

She smiles.

"That's probably why I became a teacher."

"How often did you see him?"

"Almost every weekend."

"Sometimes more."

"After Mom died..."

She shrugs.

"We checked on each other."

"How did he handle your mother's death?"

The smile disappears.

"Badly."

Not dramatically.

Simply honestly.

"He kept going."

"He functioned."

"But he wasn't happy."

"For how long?"

"Years."

"Longer than he admitted."

"Longer than most people noticed."

"Did he ever talk about it?"

"Sometimes."

"Usually in little pieces."

"Something would remind him of Mom."

"He'd get quiet."

"Then we'd talk."

"Did anything change before he disappeared?"

She nods slowly.

"Yes."

"What changed?"

"He started sleeping."

"He laughed more."

"His knees stopped bothering him as much."

She smiles faintly.

"I remember thinking..."

"Finally."

"Finally?"

"Finally he's healing."

A long silence.

"I don't think that anymore."

"Why not?"

Rebecca takes a deep breath.

"Healing takes time."

"You work through it."

"You remember."

"You cry."

"You learn to carry it."

She looks down.

"This wasn't that."

"Then what was it?"

She hesitates.

"I don't know."

Another pause.

"It was like..."

She struggles for the words.

"The grief stopped existing."

"What do you mean?"

"Not that he ignored it."

"Not that he accepted it."

"It just..."

She shakes her head.

"Wasn't there anymore."

"Wouldn't most people consider that a good thing?"

Rebecca doesn't answer immediately.

"I did."

Quietly.

"At first."

"What changed your mind?"

"Because my father noticed it."

She smiles sadly.

"Before anyone else."

"What did he say?"

She looks toward the floor.

"He told me..."

A pause.

"Grief doesn't simply disappear."

Another pause.

"If mine has..."

She closes her eyes briefly.

"Something removed it."

"Do you believe him?"

"Yes."

Without hesitation.

"I don't know what it means."

"But I believe he knows himself."

"Did you notice any other changes?"

"He seemed..."

She searches for the word.

"Lighter."

"Not happier."

"Just..."

A pause.

"Less burdened."

"How did that make you feel?"

She laughs once.

It's almost embarrassed.

"Like a terrible daughter."

"Why?"

"Because I was happy."

"I'd wanted him to feel that way for years."

She looks down.

"Then I started wondering what it had cost."

"Do you think your father is still the same man?"

Rebecca thinks for a very long time.

"Mostly."

Then quietly—

"I hope that's the right answer."

"Do you think we're asking the right questions?"

Rebecca studies the investigators carefully before answering.

"That depends."

"On what?"

"Whether you're trying to understand what happened..."

"Or whether you're trying to prove something about my father."

"Can you explain the difference?"

"Dad has spent his entire life following evidence."

"If you have evidence..."

"He'll cooperate with anything you ask."

She pauses.

"If you're asking the same questions over and over because you don't like the answers..."

She gently shakes her head.

"That's not investigation anymore."

"What worries you the most?"

"That everyone keeps asking whether Dad is happier."

She folds her hands together.

"I think we're asking the wrong question."

"What's the right question?"

She answers almost immediately.

"Who decided he shouldn't hurt anymore?"

Silence.

"Because it wasn't him."

"Do you have any concerns about our investigation?"

Rebecca hesitates.

"I do."

"What concerns you?"

"My father disappeared."

"He came home."

"He's answered every question anyone has asked him."

"He's undergone every medical test they wanted."

She folds her hands together.

"If you're looking for answers..."

"I'll help however I can."

A pause.

"If you're simply going to keep circling the same questions because you don't like the answers..."

She looks each investigator in the eye.

"I'll make sure someone asks why."

"Is there anything else we should know?"

She smiles softly.

"Dad spent his whole life teaching me to follow evidence."

"Not assumptions."

"Not wishes."

"Evidence."

She looks directly at the investigators.

"Please don't stop asking questions just because the answers look comforting."

Another pause.

"But don't keep asking them after the evidence tells you it's time to move on."

## Handler Notes

Rebecca occupies a unique role among the supporting witnesses. She is neither simply an emotional family member nor merely another source of corroborating information. As a science teacher raised by an engineer, she instinctively evaluates how investigators conduct themselves as much as what they ask.

When investigators demonstrate intellectual honesty—distinguishing observation from inference, acknowledging uncertainty, and pursuing genuinely new evidence—Rebecca becomes a valuable ally. She encourages Hal to cooperate, shares her observations freely, and respects the professionalism of the investigation.

If, however, investigators begin treating Hal as a suspect rather than a witness, repeatedly revisit settled questions without new evidence, or imply that his competence or memory is inherently suspect, Rebecca reaches a different conclusion. In her mind, the investigation has ceased to be evidence-driven and has become institutional overreach. She does not confront the agents dramatically or refuse further cooperation. Instead, she quietly does what a thoughtful citizen would do: she contacts Chief Patrick O'Connor and asks why federal investigators continue scrutinizing a seventy-one-year-old retired engineer who has already answered every question put to him.

This makes Rebecca one of the operation's clearest demonstrations of a central campaign principle: people respond to how investigators investigate. Her actions are not obstacles imposed by the Handler—they are natural consequences of the investigators' own methods. At the same time, her central ethical question remains one of the most important in the operation:

"Who decided he shouldn't hurt anymore?"

That question reframes the mystery from one of outcome to one of agency, inviting the investigators to consider whether an imposed improvement is still a gift when the person receiving it never had the opportunity to choose.
