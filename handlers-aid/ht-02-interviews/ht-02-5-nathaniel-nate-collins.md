# HT-02.5 — Interview Guide — Nathaniel “Nate” Collins

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## General Guidance

Nate approaches the interview as a collaborative investigation rather than an interrogation.

He answers directly, often qualifying statements with phrases like "I don't know," "I think," or "I'm reasonably confident."

He is comfortable saying, "I don't have enough data."

Unlike the other victims, Nate frequently distinguishes between memory, observation, and inference without being prompted. If investigators demonstrate technical competence or careful reasoning, he becomes noticeably more engaged.

## Interview
"Can you walk me through the day you disappeared?"

"Sure."

"I was off work."

"I packed my radio gear."

"Laptop."

"Portable SDR."

"Battery pack."

"Mostly I wanted to get outside for a while."

A pause.

"I also had an interference problem I'd been trying to reproduce."

"Where were you planning to go?"

"Somewhere quiet."

"Somewhere I could get away from RF noise."

He smiles slightly.

"That doesn't narrow it down much."

Another pause.

"I honestly don't remember where I decided to drive."

"That's... unusual for me."

"What was the last thing you clearly remember?"

He closes his eyes for several seconds.

"Walking."

"Checking signal strength."

"Stopping to compare readings."

"Looking at a waterfall display."

A pause.

"Everything up to that point feels sequential."

"After that..."

He opens his eyes.

"The sequence breaks."

"What do you mean?"

"Normally memories overlap."

"One leads to another."

"This one doesn't."

He gestures with one hand.

"It's like somebody deleted the middle of a log file."

"The entries before and after are intact."

"Everything between them is gone."

"Did you hear anything unusual?"

"Interference."

"Definitely."

"But that's why I was out there."

"I don't know if it was unusual before..."

A pause.

"Or only seems unusual now."

"Do you remember seeing anyone?"

"No."

He thinks.

"I don't remember seeing anyone."

"I also don't remember expecting to see anyone."

Another pause.

"Those aren't the same thing."

"Do you remember where you were?"

He answers immediately.

"No."

"I remember terrain."

"Trees."

"Uneven ground."

"That's about it."

"Did you ever feel like you were lost?"

"No."

"Disoriented afterward."

"Not while I remember being conscious."

"What do you remember next?"

"Sitting on the ground."

"Backpack beside me."

"Radio powered off."

"Battery still had charge."

He frowns.

"I know I wouldn't have shut it off."

"Why not?"

"Because if I was testing interference..."

He shrugs.

"I'd leave it recording."

"That's what bothers me."

"Were you injured?"

"No."

"Hungry."

"Dehydrated."

"Embarrassed."

"Embarrassed?"

A faint smile.

"I assumed I'd done something stupid."

"Until I checked the date."

"Did you dream?"

"Not that I remember."

A pause.

"And before you ask..."

"No."

"I don't know if that means I didn't dream."

"Have you been feeling different since you came back?"

He nods immediately.

"Yes."

"How?"

"Social situations are easier."

"Much easier."

"I still recognize the situations that used to make me anxious."

"They just..."

He searches for the right phrase.

"Don't produce the same response anymore."

"How do you know that?"

"Because I've been documenting it."

Matter-of-factly.

"Spreadsheet."

"Daily observations."

"Behavioral comparisons."

"Mostly qualitative."

"I haven't found a good way to quantify it yet."

"Do you think you're happier?"

He considers that.

"Maybe."

"That's difficult to measure."

"More comfortable?"

He nods.

"Definitely."

"Does that concern you?"

Immediately.

"Yes."

"Why?"

"Because changes should have causes."

"I know the effect."

"I don't know the mechanism."

"Until I do..."

He shrugs.

"Any conclusion would be premature."

"What do you think happened?"

He smiles politely.

"That's not a question I can answer."

"Why not?"

"Insufficient evidence."

Then he smiles again.

"Ask me what I think the evidence supports."

"What does the evidence support?"

He leans forward slightly.

"Four unrelated people disappear."

"Four return physically healthy."

"Four exhibit subtle but measurable behavioral changes."

"None remember the missing interval."

He pauses.

"That's a pattern."

"Patterns deserve investigation."

"They don't justify conclusions."

"Have you remembered anything since coming home?"

"No."

"But I've noticed things."

"Like what?"

"I keep reaching conclusions faster than I used to."

"Not because I'm thinking differently."

"Because..."

He hesitates.

"Certain kinds of uncertainty don't bother me anymore."

He looks uncomfortable.

"I miss that."

"You miss being anxious?"

He laughs quietly.

"No."

Another pause.

"I miss knowing where my personality ends and whatever happened to me begins."

"Is there anything you haven't told anyone else?"

He thinks.

Longer than any previous answer.

"I've stopped trying to recover my memory."

The investigators wait.

"At first I thought that was the important question."

"Now I think understanding the pattern matters more than remembering my own experience."

He shrugs.

"Memory is evidence."

"It's not the only evidence."

"What worries you the most?"

"That I'm asking the wrong question."

A pause.

"People keep asking me what happened to me."

He looks down at his notebook.

"I'm starting to think the better question is..."

Another pause.

"What happened to all of us?"

## Handler Notes

Nate is the closest thing the investigators have to a peer. He is already conducting his own disciplined inquiry into the disappearances, documenting behavioral changes, preserving digital records, and resisting the temptation to leap to extraordinary conclusions. Unlike Brooks, he does not search for historical analogies; unlike Hal, he does not frame the experience philosophically. His instinct is to improve the quality of the evidence before attempting to explain it.

That makes him one of the operation's most valuable interviews. The investigators leave not because Nate has solved the mystery, but because he has demonstrated the same methodological discipline the Program strives to teach: distinguish observation from inference, preserve data before interpretation, and let patterns emerge through comparison rather than certainty. In many ways, Nate is practicing Delta Green tradecraft without realizing it.
