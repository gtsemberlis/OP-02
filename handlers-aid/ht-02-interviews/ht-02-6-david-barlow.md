# HT-02.6 — Interview Guide — David Barlow

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## Default Timing

This guide assumes investigators interview Dave during Act I (June 27–July 1), after his return but before he has fully accepted that the investigation concerns his disappearance rather than his finances. Depending on how investigators approach him, the interview may begin with significant distrust before gradually becoming cooperative.

## General Guidance

Dave is naturally friendly, talkative, and good with people.

However, investigators encounter him at the worst moment of his life.

He is convinced federal agents have uncovered years of accounting fraud.

As a result, he initially interprets almost every question through the lens of financial crime.

He isn't lying because he's malicious.

He's lying because he thinks he's answering a completely different investigation.

If investigators become confrontational, Dave becomes increasingly defensive.

If investigators patiently explain that they're interested in the disappearance itself, his demeanor changes dramatically. Humor slowly returns, his shoulders relax, and he begins answering honestly.

## Interview
"Mr. Barlow, we'd like to ask about your disappearance."

His first response isn't verbal.

He studies every investigator in the room.

Trying to determine how much they already know.

Finally—

"Sure."

"What exactly do you want to know?"

"Can you walk us through that day?"

"Just another service call."

He answers too quickly.

"Couple maintenance jobs."

"Nothing special."

He immediately realizes that's incomplete.

"Then I had the day off."

"Where were you headed?"

He shrugs.

"Drive somewhere."

"Get out of the house."

"Take the radio along."

A pause.

"Couldn't tell you where now."

He laughs nervously.

"Hell of a thing to forget."

"What do you remember before you disappeared?"

"Driving."

"Walking."

"Radio making a racket."

"Then..."

He snaps his fingers.

"Nothing."

"Do you remember where you were?"

"Nope."

Immediately.

"Trees."

"Dirt."

"Outside."

He shrugs.

"That's about all I've got."

"Did you see anyone?"

"No."

He thinks.

"I don't remember seeing anybody."

"Wish I did."

"Did anything feel unusual?"

"Radio acted funny."

"Thought maybe I'd bumped the antenna."

He grins.

"Wouldn't have been the first time."

The smile fades.

"Guess it wasn't the antenna."

"What do you remember next?"

"Waking up."

"Sitting on the ground."

"Truck wasn't far away."

"Everything else..."

He shakes his head.

"Blank."

"Were you injured?"

"No."

"Hungry as hell."

"Confused."

"Did you think you'd been assaulted?"

"Honestly?"

He laughs.

"I figured maybe I'd had a stroke."

"Have you noticed any changes since coming home?"

He hesitates.

"People keep asking me that."

"What do you tell them?"

"Nothing."

Another pause.

"Mostly because I didn't notice."

"Your family says you've changed."

Dave looks uncomfortable.

"Yeah."

"Apparently."

"How?"

"Don't get mad as much."

He shrugs.

"Guess that's an improvement."

"Anything else?"

"Kids have been coming around more."

A faint smile.

"Maybe because I stopped yelling at them."

He immediately looks embarrassed.

"Didn't realize I did that as much as I did."

"Does any of that bother you?"

Long pause.

"Yeah."

"Why?"

"Because people don't usually wake up nicer."

He laughs once.

It's not a happy laugh.

"That's not how life works."

## Mid-Interview Shift

If investigators remain focused on the disappearance—and make no accusations about finances—Dave gradually becomes confused.

Finally—

"Can I ask you something?"

"Of course."

"You're really here about me disappearing?"

He looks from one investigator to another.

"That's it?"

If investigators answer honestly—

Dave sits down.

Long silence.

Then quietly:

"Well..."

"I've been an idiot."

"What do you mean?"

"Thought you were here because of my books."

He rubs his face.

"Spent the last week convincing myself I was going to prison."

"Books?"

He sighs.

"Business books."

"Taxes."

"Nothing to do with... whatever happened out there."

He looks genuinely ashamed.

"I almost made this whole thing worse."

"Why didn't you tell us?"

"Because I thought you already knew."

A tired laugh.

"Funny how scared can make a man answer questions nobody asked."

"What do you think happened to you?"

He shakes his head.

"No idea."

"And that's saying something."

"I've spent my whole life fixing things."

"Can't even figure out what broke here."

"What worries you the most?"

He answers immediately.

"That I almost missed the point."

The investigators wait.

"Whatever happened out there..."

"It scared me less than the IRS."

He laughs again.

Then stops.

"That's a hell of a thing to admit."

## If Trust Is Fully Established

Near the end of the interview, Dave quietly excuses himself.

He returns carrying a small locked case.

Without opening it immediately, he places it on the kitchen table.

"Bought this three days ago."

A long silence.

"Never owned a gun in my life."

"Never wanted one."

He opens the case.

Inside is a pristine .38 caliber revolver and a box of factory ammunition.

The revolver has clearly never been fired.

He gently slides the case toward the investigators.

"Take the damn thing."

Another silence.

"I never should've bought it."

## Handler Notes

Dave's interview is about misdirection—not from the Handler, but from the character himself. His initial evasiveness should naturally lead investigators to suspect he is concealing details about the disappearance. As the conversation unfolds, it becomes clear that he is hiding something entirely unrelated: years of financial misconduct and the desperate fear that federal agents have finally come for him.

When that misunderstanding is resolved, the emotional tone of the interview changes completely. Dave's humor returns, his defensiveness melts away, and the investigators discover that he is, beneath the fear, exactly the generous and practical man everyone describes. His eventual confession about the tax fraud is not the operation's "twist"; it is a lesson in investigative discipline. People often hide the truth, but not necessarily the truth you think they're hiding.

The revolver scene should never feel like a tactical encounter. It is a moment of surrender. Dave isn't relinquishing a weapon—he is relinquishing the illusion that fear and secrecy could protect him. That moment often becomes one of the most human scenes in the entire operation, contrasting sharply with the incomprehensible nature of the Unnatural that lies beyond it.
