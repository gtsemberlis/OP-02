# SC-03 — David Barlow Residence

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## Purpose
Introduce David Barlow, demonstrate how unrelated criminal conduct can obscure the true investigation, and challenge investigators to distinguish ordinary human wrongdoing from evidence of the Unnatural.
The investigators' approach determines whether David becomes a frightened obstacle or a valuable witness.

## Scene Overview
David Barlow's house feels orderly.
Not peaceful.
Controlled.
The lawn is trimmed.
The blinds remain closed.
Every room appears meticulously maintained.
Mail is sorted into neat stacks.
Bills are filed.
Counters are spotless.
The television quietly plays financial news or local weather, more for background reassurance than entertainment.
Nothing suggests conspiracy.
Nothing suggests madness.
Instead, the entire house reflects someone desperately trying to impose order on a life that no longer makes sense.
David wants to cooperate.
He simply believes the investigators are asking questions about the wrong thing.
He assumes the FBI has discovered his financial crimes.
The investigators know nothing about them.
That misunderstanding shapes every conversation that follows.

## What Can The Investigators Learn?
This scene demonstrates that ordinary crimes and impossible events can coexist without being connected.
David's Perspective
David remembers very little of his disappearance.
Unable to accept that something impossible occurred, he searches desperately for an ordinary explanation.
Repeated interviews convince him federal investigators must be interested in:
his taxes;
his business records;
insurance claims;
accounting irregularities.
His fear is genuine.
His explanation is entirely wrong.

The Financial Records
A review of David's records confirms exactly what he fears.
He has committed financial crimes.
Cash income has gone unreported.
Business deductions have been inflated.
Accounting practices have steadily deteriorated.
The evidence is substantial.
It is also completely unrelated to the disappearances.
That absence becomes the artifact's most important finding.
The records eliminate one of the investigation's strongest false leads by demonstrating that David is guilty—
Just not of this.

David After His Return
Friends, family, and coworkers independently describe remarkable changes following David's return.
Most noticeable:
His temper is gone.
For years, employees accepted occasional angry outbursts during stressful jobs.
Those moments simply stop.
He also:
chain-smokes less, and sometimes forgets to smoke entirely during stressful days;
apologizes more readily;
spends more time with his adult children;
remains calm during situations that once provoked frustration.
Ironically, these changes make him increasingly willing to confront the financial mistakes he spent years avoiding.
If trust has been established, he may eventually admit:
"I thought you were here because of the books."
He laughs quietly.
"I almost made everything worse trying to protect myself."
The investigators begin recognizing a familiar pattern.
The disappearances leave people subtly... improved.
That realization becomes increasingly difficult to dismiss.

Melissa's Perspective
Melissa provides the investigators with the context David cannot.
She confirms:
years of financial stress;
deteriorating bookkeeping;
increasing avoidance;
ordinary fear of federal investigation.
She also observes something deeply unsettling.
Shortly before David disappeared...
He finally became the man she remembered marrying.
More patient.
More honest.
More at peace.
She eventually says:
"He finally became the man I married."
A long pause.
"About twenty years too late."

## What Can Happen In This Scene?
These are interactive outcomes rather than pieces of information.

Interview David
David cooperates cautiously.
His answers become increasingly guarded if investigators unintentionally reinforce his belief that they are building a tax case.

Examine The Home Office
Investigators may review years of meticulously organized records.
The more thoroughly they investigate, the more clearly they discover that David's criminal conduct has nothing to do with the disappearances.

Reassure—or Pressure—David
The investigators choose how to handle knowledge of David's financial crimes.
They may reassure him that his bookkeeping is outside the scope of their investigation.
Or they may exploit his fears to obtain cooperation.
Both approaches are effective.
Neither is morally neutral.

The Wrong Investigation (Optional Pocket Scene)
If investigators confront David after learning about his financial records or recent firearm purchase, this encounter may develop into the optional pocket scene "The Wrong Investigation."
Neither side understands the other's assumptions.
David believes he is about to be arrested.
The investigators suspect he may be connected to the disappearances.
The danger comes entirely from mutual misunderstanding.
Patience and empathy rapidly de-escalate the situation.
Aggression or intimidation risk creating a crisis that never needed to exist.

## Scene Progression
### Initial Visit
David appears nervous but cooperative.
His anxiety is easily mistaken for guilt.

### Mid Investigation
Repeated interviews deepen David's misunderstanding.
He reviews years of financial paperwork, contacts his accountant, and begins searching desperately for whatever investigators have supposedly discovered.

### Late Investigation
The atmosphere has changed noticeably.
Curtains remain closed.
Boxes of financial records occupy the dining room.
David apologizes repeatedly for things the investigators have never accused him of.
A recently purchased revolver now rests nearby.
He never threatens the investigators.
Its presence is an admission of fear rather than hostility.
Once David finally understands that the investigation concerns his disappearance—not his accounting—his posture changes almost immediately.
Relief replaces suspicion.
He quietly slides the revolver across the table.
"Take the damn thing."
After a long silence:
"I never should've bought it."

## Handler Notes
David Barlow's residence represents misdirected suspicion.
This is the operation's first major lesson that investigators can be entirely correct about a person's guilt while being completely wrong about its relevance. David has committed real financial crimes. His fear of federal investigators is entirely justified. His behavior is suspicious because he is hiding something—but not the thing the investigators were sent to uncover.
This scene reinforces another foundational principle of Operation Carrier Wave:
Not every secret is the secret the investigators are looking for.
The optional pocket scene, "The Wrong Investigation," should never become an action encounter. Its tension comes from misunderstanding rather than malice. David is frightened, inexperienced, and overwhelmed. The recently purchased revolver is not the weapon of an antagonist—it is the desperate comfort of a man convinced his life is about to collapse.
Like Christopher's workshop and Hal's radio logs, David's meticulously organized records reveal character more than clues. Every labeled folder, reconciled account, and carefully maintained file represents an attempt to restore order after an experience that fundamentally defied reason.
By the end of the scene, investigators should leave having learned something more important than the contents of David's books:
Good investigators do not simply uncover hidden truths.
They determine which truths actually matter.
