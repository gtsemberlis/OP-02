# SC-07 — Brooks’ Motel Room

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## Purpose

Introduce Evan Brooks as an independent investigator whose greatest contribution is comparative analysis rather than privileged information.

The motel room demonstrates how a disciplined civilian investigator can reconstruct much of the operation using only public information, while introducing historical parallels that encourage investigators to think beyond the current case.

## Scene Overview

From the parking lot, Brooks' room appears indistinguishable from every other motel room.

A faded exterior door.

A single window.

A weathered air conditioner humming beneath the sill.

Inside, however, the room has ceased being temporary lodging.

It has become a research office.

Every available surface holds evidence.

Maps cover the bed.

Field notebooks occupy the desk.

Two laptops remain open beside external hard drives, radios, charging cables, coffee cups, and stacks of printed newspaper articles.

Sticky notes connect disappearances across decades.

The television silently displays local news.

A police scanner occasionally interrupts the quiet.

Nothing here resembles the stereotypical conspiracy theorist's lair.

The room feels intensely professional.

Simply underfunded.

The investigators may experience an uncomfortable realization.

Given another week...

...their own temporary headquarters might begin looking very similar.

## What Can The Investigators Learn?

This scene demonstrates that independent investigators can reach many of the same conclusions through entirely different methods.

Brooks' Perspective

Brooks believes extraordinary claims require extraordinary documentation.

He does not collect stories.

He collects patterns.

Every newspaper clipping.

Every FOIA response.

Every interview.

Every radio recording.

Every map.

Every contradiction.

He continually revises earlier conclusions as new evidence becomes available.

The investigators may disagree with his explanation.

They cannot honestly dismiss the quality of his work.

Comparative Cases

Brooks has documented five historical disappearances that strongly resemble the current investigation.

Across multiple decades and several states he has identified recurring characteristics:

three to five victims;
disappearances over a short period;
later return with missing time;
subtle but lasting personality changes;
one or two victims never returning.

Brooks believes these represent alien abductions.

The investigators recognize something different.

Not proof.

A pattern.

For the first time, the operation stops feeling unique.

Brooks' Interview Method

Perhaps Brooks' greatest contribution is not his archive.

It is the questions he asks.

Unlike conventional investigators, Brooks is comfortable with incomplete memories.

He asks about:

sounds;
terrain;
temperature;
elevation;
fatigue;
orientation;
fleeting sensory impressions.

He rarely insists on chronology.

He never pressures witnesses into certainty.

That patience recovers details official interviews often miss.

Investigators may ultimately reject Brooks' conclusions while quietly adopting his interview techniques.

Nate's Report—Revisited

Brooks' annotated copy of Nate Collins' report demonstrates how comparative analysis changes interpretation.

Nothing in Nate's original report has changed.

Brooks simply notices relationships between Nate's observations and decades of similar cases.

His handwritten notes consistently distinguish:

confirmed facts;
recurring patterns;
tentative hypotheses.

The annotations reinforce Brooks' greatest strength.

He is willing to abandon ideas when evidence no longer supports them.

## What Can Happen In This Scene?

These are interactive outcomes rather than pieces of information.

Interview Brooks

Brooks happily discusses methodology.

He is far less interested in persuading investigators of extraterrestrial explanations than in comparing evidence.

Respectful disagreement strengthens rapport.

Dismissiveness does not.

Examine The Archive

Investigators may spend hours reviewing decades of accumulated research.

Most of the archive duplicates public information.

Its real value lies in organization.

Patterns emerge that no single jurisdiction would have recognized independently.

Compare Investigative Approaches

Brooks eagerly compares his work with the investigators'.

He immediately identifies similarities.

Differences.

Missing questions.

Overlooked assumptions.

The conversation often becomes more valuable than the documents themselves.

Decide Whether To Cooperate

The investigators may choose to treat Brooks as:

a nuisance;
a security concern;
an intelligence source;
a fellow investigator.

That decision shapes the remainder of their relationship.

Brooks never stops investigating.

The only question is whether the investigators understand what he is learning.

## Scene Progression
### Initial Visit

Brooks' room appears cluttered.

Closer examination reveals extraordinary organization.

His work mirrors much of the official investigation despite relying almost entirely on public information.

### Mid Investigation

Speculation gradually disappears.

Maps increasingly focus on Hogsback State Forest.

Radio recordings occupy more of Brooks' attention.

Historical comparisons become increasingly precise.

His investigation begins converging with the investigators' own conclusions from an entirely independent direction.

### Late Investigation

The motel room has become SaucerWatch's operational hub.

Incoming reports arrive faster than Brooks can catalog them.

Volunteers contribute maps, recordings, and photographs.

Most submissions are noise.

Some prove remarkably valuable.

If Daniel Kowalski has disappeared, his photograph now appears alongside the previous victims.

Brooks studies the board before quietly saying:

"I don't think this is escalating."

A pause.

"I think we're finally seeing enough of it to recognize what it's always been."

## Handler Notes

Brooks' motel room represents comparative inquiry.

Where Hal Whitaker teaches investigators to observe, David Barlow reminds them to distinguish relevant evidence from unrelated guilt, Christopher Donnelly models systems thinking, Samantha Carter humanizes the victims, and Nathaniel Collins demonstrates disciplined documentation, Brooks reveals the power of looking beyond a single case.

His archive is valuable not because it contains hidden truths, but because it preserves institutional memory that no institution ever assembled. By connecting disappearances separated by decades and geography, Brooks reaches an insight unavailable to investigators focused solely on Oneida County: the pattern predates the current operation.

Brooks is intentionally wrong about the explanation.

He interprets recurring evidence through the cultural framework available to him—alien abduction.

That error should never obscure the quality of his investigation. His methodology is exemplary. He distinguishes facts from speculation, revises conclusions when evidence changes, and allows witnesses to remain uncertain rather than forcing coherent narratives from incomplete memories.

This scene introduces another core investigative principle of Operation Carrier Wave:

The quality of an investigation is determined less by the answers it produces than by the questions it asks.

Brooks is not a Delta Green agent.

He has no access to classified information, institutional authority, or historical archives.

Yet through patience, intellectual honesty, and years of disciplined comparison, he independently approaches many of the same conclusions as the Program. In doing so, he becomes an uncomfortable mirror for the investigators—a reminder that truth is not the exclusive property of official institutions.
