# SC-06 — Nate Collins Apartment

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## Purpose
Introduce Nathaniel Collins as the investigation's most technically sophisticated civilian witness, provide the operation's strongest collection of independently verifiable digital evidence, and establish the moment the investigation begins spreading beyond official channels.
The investigators' interaction with Nate directly influences the emergence of SaucerWatch as an active participant in the operation.

## Scene Overview
Nate's apartment feels immediately different from every previous location.
The space belongs to someone who enjoys technology.
Computer fans hum quietly.
Multiple monitors glow across a large desk.
One displays a software-defined radio waterfall.
Another contains GIS mapping software.
A third cycles between photographs, browser tabs, and technical documentation.
Coffee mugs occupy nearly every available surface.
Portable radios.
GPS receivers.
Camera equipment.
Drone batteries.
Field notebooks.
Everything has been used recently.
Unlike David's meticulous organization, Nate's workspace feels alive.
Unlike Christopher's workshop, it isn't devoted to solving a single problem.
It reflects years of curiosity.
The investigators quickly realize they are looking at someone who documents the world simply because he enjoys understanding how it works.
That habit has unexpectedly preserved some of the investigation's most valuable evidence.

## What Can The Investigators Learn?
This scene demonstrates that modern technology remembers even when people cannot.
Nate's Perspective
Nate approaches his disappearance as a technical problem.
He does not attempt to prove extraordinary explanations.
Instead, he attempts to reconcile conflicting datasets.
His central question is remarkably simple:
"If my memory is incomplete, what do my devices remember?"
That question leads him toward evidence unavailable anywhere else.

Digital Evidence
Multiple independent systems recorded anomalies surrounding Nate's disappearance.
Investigators may compare:
SDR recordings;
GPS tracks;
camera timestamps;
cellular location history;
fitness tracker data;
photographs;
computer logs.
No individual dataset explains what happened.
Together they demonstrate that something physically measurable occurred.
The missing time exists not only in memory.
It exists within the digital record itself.

Nate After His Return
Coworkers notice the changes before Nate does.
Students describe him as more approachable.
Faculty notice he voluntarily joins conversations he once avoided.
For years, Nate quietly struggled with social anxiety.
After returning, that anxiety simply...
stops.
When investigators ask how he feels, he answers thoughtfully.
"It's strange."
A pause.
"I still know what anxiety feels like."
Another pause.
"I just... don't experience it anymore."
Unlike Christopher or Hal, Nate immediately begins documenting the change.
He creates spreadsheets.
Behavioral comparisons.
Daily observations.
He treats himself as another dataset requiring analysis.
Like the other victims, he recognizes the change.
Unlike the others, his instinct is to quantify it.

The SaucerWatch Report
Eventually Nate organizes his observations into a structured investigative report.
The report deliberately distinguishes:
observations;
evidence;
assumptions;
speculation.
Rather than claiming extraordinary conclusions, Nate repeatedly acknowledges uncertainty.
Its greatest strength is not the explanation it offers.
It is the discipline with which it refuses to overstate the evidence.
That methodological rigor is precisely what attracts Evan Brooks' attention.

## What Can Happen In This Scene?
These are interactive outcomes rather than pieces of information.

Interview Nate
Nate enthusiastically demonstrates his equipment.
He enjoys explaining methodology.
He never exaggerates his conclusions.
If investigators challenge his assumptions with better evidence, he revises them immediately.

Examine Digital Evidence
Investigators may spend hours reviewing recordings, metadata, GPS histories, photographs, software logs, and backups.
Every dataset independently corroborates part of the investigation.
Together they create the operation's strongest body of civilian forensic evidence.

Compare Methodologies
Nate eagerly compares his documentation with evidence gathered elsewhere.
He immediately recognizes the value of Hal's recordings.
Christopher's engineering analyses.
Samantha's observations.
Rather than competing with other witnesses, his work strengthens theirs.

Discuss Publication
Investigators may encourage—or discourage—Nate from sharing his findings publicly.
He has no interest in becoming famous.
He simply believes other technically competent people may recognize patterns he has missed.
Whether the investigators support or oppose that decision, Nate ultimately chooses to share his work.
That decision fundamentally changes the scope of the investigation.

## Scene Progression
### Initial Visit
Nate shares everything.
His apartment contains the investigation's richest collection of digital evidence.
He is relieved that someone with professional resources is finally taking his observations seriously.

### Mid Investigation
Following the investigators' visit, Nate organizes his findings into a comprehensive report.
He uploads the material to SaucerWatch.
Within hours, responses begin arriving from around the country.
Most are unhelpful.
A handful demonstrate genuine technical competence.
One of them belongs to Evan Brooks.

### Late Investigation
The apartment has become noticeably busier.
Browser tabs multiply.
Maps cover the desk.
Email notifications arrive constantly.
Video calls with Brooks may already be underway.
Nate is energized—not because he believes he has solved the mystery, but because, for the first time, he is collaborating with people who value evidence as much as he does.
Without intending to, he has transformed a private investigation into a public one.

## Handler Notes
Nate Collins' apartment represents evidence preservation.
Where Hal Whitaker teaches investigators to observe, Christopher Donnelly models systems thinking, and David Barlow warns against false conclusions, Nate demonstrates the extraordinary investigative power of ordinary technology. Modern life is saturated with sensors—GPS receivers, digital cameras, software-defined radios, smartphones, drones, and networked computers. None were designed to detect the Unnatural. Together, they preserve traces of phenomena their owners cannot explain.
Nate also embodies another of the campaign's defining principles:
Good investigators distinguish observations from explanations.
His SaucerWatch report earns Brooks' respect not because it reaches the correct conclusion—it doesn't—but because Nate consistently separates facts, interpretations, assumptions, and hypotheses. He revises his thinking when confronted with better evidence and openly acknowledges uncertainty. In many respects, he investigates exactly as a Delta Green agent should.
This scene also marks a major turning point in the operation.
Until now, the investigation has remained largely contained within the FBI, local authorities, and the Program.
When Nate chooses to publish his evidence, that containment quietly ends.
He does not compromise the investigation through recklessness or sensationalism.
He compromises it by behaving exactly as a careful, intellectually honest citizen should: documenting his evidence and sharing it with others capable of evaluating it.
That decision brings Evan Brooks—and eventually SaucerWatch itself—into the operation, expanding the investigation beyond the control of any official agency.
