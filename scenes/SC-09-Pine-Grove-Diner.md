# SC-09 — Pine Grove Diner

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## Purpose

Introduce Richard "Rick" Holloway, president of the Mohawk Valley Amateur Radio Club, and provide investigators with the community perspective on the disappearances.

The Pine Grove Diner demonstrates that the victims were connected through ordinary friendship rather than secret conspiracy, while showing how an entire community quietly adapts to repeated tragedy.

## Scene Overview

The Pine Grove Diner sits just outside New Hartford along the highway leading toward Hogsback State Forest.

It isn't famous.

It isn't picturesque.

It's simply where people end up.

Hunters eat breakfast here before heading into the woods.

Deputies stop for coffee between calls.

Park rangers know the waitresses by name.

Search volunteers gather here after long days on the trails.

If something happens around Hogsback, someone at the Pine Grove has already heard about it.

The investigators arrive to familiar sounds.

Coffee cups clink.

Orders are called into the kitchen.

Local news plays quietly on a television mounted above the counter.

Conversations pause briefly as strangers enter.

Then resume.

The room never feels suspicious.

Only curious.

Within minutes, someone quietly points toward a booth.

"You're probably looking for Rick."

## What Can The Investigators Learn?

This scene demonstrates that shared community is not evidence of conspiracy.

Rick's Perspective

Rick understands all five victims.

Not because he investigated them.

Because he knew them.

When investigators begin asking about the Amateur Radio Club, Rick doesn't produce theories.

He tells stories.

Chris always volunteered to repair club equipment.

Hal patiently mentored new operators.

Dave never missed Field Day.

Samantha asked thoughtful questions.

Nate quietly solved everyone else's technical problems.

Rick never describes them as a secret group.

He describes them as friends.

That distinction matters.

The investigators begin to understand that the Amateur Radio Club is not an organization of suspects.

It is simply a community that happened to be struck repeatedly by tragedy.

The Empty Chairs

Rick quietly admits something he has told no one else.

He keeps attendance records for every club meeting.

Not because the club requires them.

Because he likes remembering who was there.

When the disappearances began...

He noticed the empty chairs.

One after another.

Chris.

Hal.

Dave.

Samantha.

Nate.

By the fifth disappearance he found himself staring at five empty seats before realizing the meeting had already begun.

He never told anyone.

He simply couldn't stop noticing them.

The investigators realize they have been counting victims.

Rick has been counting friends.

Community Memory

The Pine Grove rarely answers direct questions.

Instead, it answers questions investigators didn't know to ask.

Through conversations with Maggie and the regulars, investigators may learn:

how the town's attitude has changed after each disappearance;
which search areas locals considered most difficult;
who has recently been asking unusual questions;
which trails people actually use;
how search efforts were organized;
how fear has gradually become routine.

Unlike institutional records, these conversations preserve the emotional history of the investigation.

Maggie's Perspective

Maggie remembers people the way only a longtime diner owner can.

Not by formal records.

By habits.

Who always ordered rye toast.

Who switched to decaf.

Who stopped coming.

She rarely offers conclusions.

Instead, she quietly recalls details everyone else has forgotten.

Her observations help investigators reconstruct ordinary routines that disappeared alongside the victims.

These are interactive outcomes rather than pieces of information.

Interview Rick

Rick gladly discusses every victim.

Review Club Records

These establish years of ordinary relationships without suggesting conspiracy.

Listen To The Community

The investigators may spend time simply talking with patrons.

No single conversation changes the investigation.

Taken together, dozens of small observations paint an increasingly complete picture of how Hogsback has affected the town.

Become Familiar Faces

Repeated visits change the investigators' relationship with the diner.

People stop lowering their voices.

Someone offers coffee before they ask.

Regulars begin approaching their booth with observations they hadn't considered important.

Trust develops naturally.

The diner quietly becomes an extension of the investigation.

## Scene Progression
### Initial Visit

Rick provides the investigators with the human history of the Amateur Radio Club.

The disappearances still feel like individual tragedies.

### Mid Investigation

Conversations increasingly focus on Hogsback.

Search volunteers share observations.

Community optimism begins fading into quiet resignation.

The investigators become familiar faces rather than outsiders.

### Late Investigation

SaucerWatch has arrived.

The parking lot contains unfamiliar vehicles.

Maps cover breakfast tables.

Portable radios crackle between booths.

If Daniel Kowalski has disappeared, exhausted search volunteers arrive covered in dirt and quietly drink coffee before returning to the forest.

Maggie keeps refilling cups.

Eventually she remarks:

"Business is up."

A pause.

"I'd rather have my old customers back."

## Handler Notes

The Pine Grove Diner represents community memory.

Every other major location preserves information through systems: reports, recordings, journals, digital archives, or investigative files. The diner preserves information through ordinary human relationships. It remembers people by routines, conversations, favorite meals, and empty chairs.

Rick Holloway serves a similar purpose. Unlike the FBI, the Program, or SaucerWatch, he never knew the victims as cases. He knew them as neighbors. His stories restore individuality to people the investigation risks reducing to evidence.

Equally important, Rick dismantles one of the operation's easiest false narratives. The victims shared a hobby, but they were not members of a hidden organization or clandestine project. Their connection was ordinary. The Amateur Radio Club exists because people enjoy radios and each other's company, not because they were searching for the Unnatural.

This scene reinforces another core principle of Operation Carrier Wave:

Shared interests are not evidence of conspiracy.

Investigators should leave the Pine Grove with fewer theories and a deeper appreciation for what has actually been lost. The disappearances are not merely interruptions in a timeline or entries in a case file. They are absences in a community. Rick notices them every time he looks at the club's attendance sheet. Maggie notices them every morning when familiar customers fail to walk through the door. That quiet accumulation of absence gives the investigation its emotional weight.
