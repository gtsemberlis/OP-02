# SC-13 — Hogsback Ranger Station

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## Purpose

Introduce Daniel Kowalski and the professional stewardship of Hogsback State Forest, establishing the environmental baseline for the investigation.

The Ranger Station demonstrates how experienced forest professionals understand the landscape, while Daniel's disappearance transforms the operation from an investigation of past events into an active search for someone the investigators might still save.

## Scene Overview

The Hogsback Ranger Station sits just beyond the public entrance where paved roads give way to gravel service routes winding into the forest.

Unlike the nearby trailhead, this is not a visitor destination.

It is a workplace.

Mud-covered pickup trucks occupy the parking lot.

ATVs sit beside trailers loaded with rescue equipment.

Handheld radios exchange short, professional conversations between rangers patrolling miles apart.

Inside, nearly every wall is covered with maps.

Trail maintenance.

Fire history.

Wildlife surveys.

Old search operations.

Forestry management.

The building smells faintly of coffee, wet boots, pine needles, gasoline, and chain oil.

Nothing here is decorative.

Everything exists because someone uses it.

The investigators quickly recognize another recurring theme of the operation.

The people who work here know Hogsback intimately.

If they cannot explain what is happening...

...it is not because they failed to notice it.

## What Can The Investigators Learn?

This scene teaches investigators how professionals read the forest.

Daniel's Perspective

Daniel Kowalski knows Hogsback better than almost anyone.

He has walked nearly every trail.

Participated in countless rescues.

Patrolled remote sections of forest that few visitors ever see.

When investigators point toward something unusual, Daniel almost always provides an ordinary explanation.

Animal sign.

Weather.

Topography.

Old logging activity.

Radio dead zones.

His confidence is reassuring.

Precisely because it is earned.

As the investigation progresses, however, even Daniel begins encountering observations he cannot comfortably explain.

He never jumps to extraordinary conclusions.

He simply begins asking better questions.

Understanding Hogsback

The Ranger Station provides investigators with a professional understanding of the landscape.

Investigators may learn:

seasonal changes;
wildlife movement;
search methodology;
communications coverage;
difficult terrain;
historical rescue locations;
maintenance roads;
areas commonly visited by hikers.

The forest becomes less mysterious.

At least initially.

That clarity makes later events considerably more unsettling.

The Anomalous Signal

If investigators speak with Daniel before his disappearance, he casually mentions persistent radio interference affecting the northern portion of the forest.

He assumes ordinary causes.

A damaged repeater.

Atmospheric conditions.

Faulty equipment.

He demonstrates the interference without concern.

Beneath otherwise routine communications, investigators hear a faint rhythmic buzzing.

To Daniel, it is merely another maintenance problem.

To the investigators, it may become one of the operation's earliest measurable clues.

Institutional Memory

Unlike Brooks' comparative archive or the police department's case files, the Ranger Station preserves the history of the landscape itself.

Decades of patrol journals, forestry surveys, trail maps, weather observations, and rescue documentation reveal remarkable environmental stability.

The forest has changed very little.

The disappearances have.

That contrast quietly shifts the investigators' attention away from Hogsback as a place and toward whatever has begun acting within it.

## What Can Happen In This Scene?

These are interactive outcomes rather than pieces of information.

Interview Daniel

Daniel willingly answers questions about the forest.

He patiently translates unfamiliar terrain into understandable patterns and gently corrects mistaken assumptions.

His confidence encourages investigators to trust his judgment.

Review Ranger Records

Historical maps and patrol journals establish the environmental baseline against which later anomalies become meaningful.

Examine Communications

Investigators may review radio logs and compare the anomalous interference with recordings gathered elsewhere.

Each comparison strengthens the conclusion that the signal represents a genuine physical phenomenon rather than equipment malfunction.

Return After Daniel Disappears

The station becomes an entirely different place.

Investigators now confront the consequences of losing the one person who understood the forest best.

## Scene Progression
### Initial Visit

The Ranger Station is calm and professional.

Daniel serves as the investigators' guide through Hogsback, translating years of practical experience into useful context.

The forest still appears understandable.

### Mid Investigation

Daniel becomes increasingly invested in the case.

He rereads old patrol notes.

Compares historical maps.

Conducts additional observations during routine patrols.

He never claims something impossible is occurring.

He simply begins noticing more questions than answers.

### Late Investigation

Daniel Kowalski is gone.

His patrol truck remains outside.

His coffee mug still sits beside unfinished paperwork.

His notebook remains open where he left it.

No one has moved any of it.

Not intentionally.

The atmosphere has shifted from quiet professionalism to restrained grief.

Search maps dominate every wall.

Fresh grease-pencil markings document helicopter flights, K9 deployments, volunteer assignments, and expanding search sectors.

One veteran ranger studies Daniel's photograph before quietly saying:

"Visitors get lost."

A long pause.

"Danny didn't."

The investigators immediately understand the implication.

There is no plausible explanation in which Daniel simply wandered off a trail he had patrolled hundreds of times.

## Handler Notes

The Hogsback Ranger Station represents professional knowledge of place.

Every major institution in Operation Carrier Wave understands a different aspect of reality. The FBI understands evidence. The police understand communities. Brooks understands historical patterns. The Search Command Post demonstrates coordinated action. The ranger staff understand the landscape itself. Their expertise establishes the operation's environmental baseline, allowing investigators to distinguish genuinely anomalous observations from the countless ordinary phenomena encountered in any forest.

Daniel Kowalski embodies that expertise. Throughout the first half of the operation he serves as the investigators' interpreter of Hogsback, translating unfamiliar terrain into practical knowledge. His calm confidence reassures both investigators and players that the forest is knowable.

That confidence is precisely why his disappearance is so powerful.

When Daniel vanishes, the investigators do not merely lose another witness. They lose the operation's most reliable interpreter of the landscape. From this point forward, the forest ceases to behave according to the expectations of the people who know it best.

The station also introduces two of the operation's most significant artifacts: Daniel Kowalski's recovered ranger radio and his final recorded transmission. Neither reveals the truth directly. Instead, they reinforce one of the campaign's central themes:

The Unnatural can be measured long before it can be understood.

By the time investigators leave the Ranger Station for the final time, they should understand one unavoidable reality:

If the ranger who knew every trail could disappear without explanation, the investigators can no longer rely on the forest to make sense on its own.
