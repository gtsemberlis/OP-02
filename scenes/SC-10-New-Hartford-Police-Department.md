# SC-10 — New Hartford Police Department

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

## Purpose

Introduce Chief Patrick O'Connor and the local law enforcement investigation into the disappearances.

The department provides investigators with the operational history of the case before federal involvement while demonstrating the enormous effort already invested by a competent police agency confronting an impossible mystery.

## Scene Overview

The New Hartford Police Department sits beside Town Hall in a modern municipal building.

Unlike the quiet order of the FBI Field Office, this building never truly stops moving.

Dispatch radios chatter continuously.

Patrol cars enter and leave through the rear garage.

Phones ring.

Officers move between interview rooms, evidence lockers, and briefing spaces.

Coffee sits half-finished beside active reports.

Children's drawings from community outreach programs hang beside department commendations.

This is not simply an investigative headquarters.

It is part of the town itself.

The investigators quickly recognize something important.

These officers don't merely police New Hartford.

They live here.

They coach Little League.

Attend local churches.

Shop beside the victims' families.

Every disappearance has become personal.

The case isn't simply unsolved.

It's unfinished.

## What Can The Investigators Learn?

This scene demonstrates how a capable local police department responds when ordinary investigative methods reach their limits.

Chief O'Connor's Perspective

Patrick O'Connor has spent nearly twenty-five years serving New Hartford.

He knows every victim by name.

He attended family meetings.

Walked search lines.

Spoke at press conferences.

Unlike the FBI, he has lived every stage of the investigation alongside the community.

He never suggests extraordinary explanations.

Instead, he quietly asks:

"What else do you need from us?"

His instinct is never to defend his department.

It is to help solve the case.

The Local Investigation

The department preserves the investigation as it existed before federal involvement.

Investigators may reconstruct:

the original emergency calls;
patrol responses;
witness interviews;
search planning;
volunteer coordination;
mutual aid requests;
evidence collection;
chain of custody.

The records reveal a disciplined, methodical investigation.

Nothing obvious was missed.

The mystery survives despite competent police work—not because of its absence.

Operational Memory

The Operations Room tells a story no report fully captures.

Colored pins spread across county maps.

Search sectors outlined in marker.

Volunteer rosters.

Helicopter flight paths.

Dry-erase timelines updated over months.

Everything reflects an investigation that evolved organically as each disappearance expanded the scope of the response.

Unlike Brooks' archive, these materials were never assembled to identify patterns.

They exist to coordinate people trying to bring neighbors home.

That difference shapes every decision made within the room.

The Weight of Repetition

O'Connor remembers how the department changed.

The first disappearance generated optimism.

The second created concern.

The third demanded additional resources.

By the fifth...

Every officer quietly dreaded another emergency call from Hogsback.

Not because they feared the forest.

Because they feared another family would join the others waiting for answers.

The investigators realize the disappearances have exhausted not only the victims' families, but the institutions trying to protect them.

## Scene Progression
### Initial Visit

The department welcomes the investigators.

O'Connor openly shares the history of the local investigation and provides access to operational records.

Hope remains that fresh eyes may discover something previously overlooked.

### Mid Investigation

New interview summaries appear.

Old evidence is reexamined.

O'Connor quietly instructs detectives to revisit portions of the case based upon information supplied by the investigators.

Rebecca Whitaker contacts him, worried that repeated interviews are becoming harmful.

O'Connor now balances investigative necessity with the well-being of families he has known for years.

### Late Investigation

The atmosphere grows noticeably heavier.

If SaucerWatch has arrived, officers spend increasing time managing curious civilians, journalists, drone operators, and independent researchers rather than pursuing investigative leads.

If Daniel Kowalski disappears, the Operations Room transforms into an active incident command center.

Fresh search grids cover every wall.

Helicopter assignments update continuously.

Dispatch traffic becomes constant.

The department is no longer investigating a mystery.

It is racing against time to save a life.

Reviewing the updated case board, O'Connor eventually pauses before the photographs of the victims.

"I've worked murders."

A long silence.

"You eventually understand what happened."

He studies the map.

"This..."

Another pause.

"...doesn't want to be understood."

It is the closest he has come to admitting that ordinary policing may not be enough.

## Handler Notes

The New Hartford Police Department represents institutional commitment.

This scene is deliberately written to reject a common investigative trope: local law enforcement is not incompetent, corrupt, or obstructive. Chief Patrick O'Connor and his officers have pursued the disappearances with professionalism, compassion, and persistence. The evidence has simply led them to the edge of what conventional policing can explain.

O'Connor serves as the investigators' strongest local ally because his priorities mirror their own. He wants the victims found, the truth discovered, and his community protected. His willingness to share resources without defensiveness reinforces that the Program succeeds not by replacing local institutions, but by building upon the work they have already done.

The department also reveals the cumulative cost of repeated investigations. Search maps, handwritten notes, volunteer rosters, and exhausted officers remind the investigators that every disappearance leaves behind more than evidence—it consumes time, hope, and emotional resilience. By the time the agents arrive, New Hartford has already given this case months of effort.

This scene reinforces another core principle of Operation Carrier Wave:

The Unnatural does not triumph because institutions fail. It triumphs because it lies beyond the assumptions those institutions were built to test.
