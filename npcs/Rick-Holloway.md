Public Record

Richard Holloway was born in Utica, New York.

After graduating from high school, he entered the family business selling and servicing office equipment.

Over four decades he expanded the company from typewriter repair into copier systems, networking, and office technology.

He gradually stepped away from day-to-day operations, allowing his daughter to assume management while remaining active in the community.

Rick has been involved in amateur radio since his teenage years.

He has served as:

Field Day coordinator

Known throughout central New York as an enthusiastic ambassador for amateur radio.

No criminal history.

Biography

Rick believes hobbies exist to bring people together.

He has spent decades introducing strangers to amateur radio.

He remembers almost everyone who earns a license through the club.

He enjoys connecting experienced operators with newcomers who share similar interests.

Many members privately joke that Rick recruits people into friendships almost as enthusiastically as he recruits them into the hobby.

His greatest pride is not the club's equipment.

It's the people who keep coming back.

Private Context

Rick quietly keeps attendance records from every club meeting.

Officially they're for membership reporting.

Unofficially they're reminders of friendships built over decades.

When the disappearances begin, he notices empty chairs long before anyone notices a pattern.

First Chris.

Then Hal.

Then Dave.

Then Samantha.

Finally Nate.

By the fifth meeting, he finds himself staring at five empty seats before realizing the meeting has already begun.

He never tells anyone.

Under Pressure

Initially attempts to maintain normalcy.

Becomes increasingly protective of club members.

Quietly begins checking on people between meetings.

As the disappearances continue, he struggles to hide his growing fear that someone is targeting the club.

Even then, he avoids panic.

His instinct is always to take care of others first.

Those relationships become invaluable in reconstructing the victims' shared history.

Operational & Mechanical Notes

Rick's testimony is unusual because it contains almost no forensic evidence.

Instead, he provides context.

He is the first person investigators meet who naturally speaks about all five victims in the same conversation.

His interviews establish:

Chris was always fixing club equipment.
Hal mentored nearly everyone.
Dave never missed Field Day.
Samantha asked the best questions.
Nate quietly solved everyone's technical problems.

He repeatedly emphasizes that there was nothing unusual about the relationships among them.

They weren't a secret group.

They weren't conducting mysterious experiments.

They were simply members of the same community.

This reinforces one of Episode 2's central investigative principles:

Shared interests are not evidence of conspiracy.

Design Notes

Rick Holloway represents community.

Every other major NPC understands the victims through a professional lens:

Chief O'Connor sees case files.
Special Agent Alvarez sees evidence.
Dean Sullivan sees students and employees.
Families see loved ones.

Rick sees neighbors.

His interviews remind the investigators—and the players—that these disappearances are not merely investigative problems. They are absences. Every empty chair at a club meeting, every unanswered radio call, and every name missing from the attendance sheet quietly testifies to lives interrupted.

For that reason, Rick should rarely provide critical clues. His purpose is more important than that. He gives the victims humanity, making them feel like members of a real community rather than entries on a timeline. I think that emotional grounding will make the eventual revelation about what happened to them resonate far more strongly than any single piece of evidence could.
