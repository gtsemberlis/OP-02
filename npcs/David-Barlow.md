# PER-03 — David Allen Barlow

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

> Status: LOCKED CANONICAL NPC

Victim 3 — David "Dave" Barlow
Basic Information

Full Name: David Allen Barlow

Known As: "Dave"

Age: 56

Occupation: Owner, Barlow Heating & Cooling LLC

Residence: Frankfort, New York

Marital Status: Divorced

Family:

Ex-wife: Karen Barlow (53)
Son: Matthew Barlow (28), Union Electrician
Daughter: Ashley Barlow (24), Veterinary Technician

Licensed Amateur Radio Operator (General Class)

Club member for approximately fifteen years.

## Public Record

David Barlow was born in Herkimer, New York.

Graduated from Herkimer High School.

Completed an HVAC apprenticeship before earning state licensing.

Worked for regional mechanical contractors before founding Barlow Heating & Cooling LLC approximately twenty-three years ago.

Business specializes in residential HVAC installation, commercial maintenance, refrigeration repair, and emergency service calls.

Well known throughout the Mohawk Valley.

Regular participant in county emergency communications exercises through the amateur radio club.

Volunteer supporter of local youth sports and community fundraisers.

No felony criminal history.

Minor traffic violations only.

Several civil disputes involving unpaid invoices and contract disagreements, all settled or dismissed.

Financial records indicate moderate business debt consistent with a small contracting company.

## Physical Description

Height: 5'11"

Weight: 238 lbs

Heavyset but physically powerful.

Broad shoulders.

Weathered complexion from decades working outdoors.

Dark hair turning silver.

Close-cropped beard.

Large hands marked by numerous cuts and burns.

Usually wears work boots, jeans, company polo shirts, and a faded baseball cap.

Often smells faintly of refrigerant oil and cigarette smoke.

## Biography

Dave built his business through reputation rather than advertising.

He knows hundreds of customers by first name.

He's the sort of contractor people recommend because he answers the phone at 2:00 AM during the middle of winter.

Employees describe him as demanding but loyal.

Friends describe him as generous.

His marriage deteriorated under the pressures of long work hours and financial stress, ending amicably nearly a decade ago.

Since then, work has largely become his identity.

Amateur radio provides one of the few activities unrelated to his business.

Unlike many club members, Dave enjoys the social side as much as the technical aspects.

He is often the first to volunteer when equipment needs transporting, towers need erecting, or Field Day requires logistics.

## Private Context

Dave's business has quietly struggled for years.

Rising equipment costs.

Seasonal cash flow.

Increasing insurance premiums.

Delayed customer payments.

Rather than admit failure, he began manipulating the books.

Initially small.

Cash jobs left unreported.

Personal expenses classified as business purchases.

Payroll adjustments.

Eventually...

False deductions.

Inflated expenses.

Improper depreciation.

Nothing sophisticated.

Nothing violent.

Enough to become serious federal tax fraud if uncovered.

He rationalizes every decision.

"I'll fix it next year."

He never does.

Only his accountant suspects the full extent of the discrepancies.

Cognitive Signature

Highly practical.

Rapid decision-maker.

Excellent at diagnosing mechanical failures with incomplete information.

Relies heavily on experience rather than formal analysis.

Comfortable improvising solutions.

Very good at reading people.

Prefers action over discussion.

## Communication Style

Friendly.

Talkative.

Humorous.

Uses storytelling to explain nearly everything.

Frequently interrupts himself with side anecdotes.

Good at putting nervous people at ease.

Rarely uses technical jargon unless speaking with another tradesperson.

## Under Pressure

Initially becomes more energetic.

Moves constantly.

Begins solving problems immediately.

If he believes events are outside his control, frustration quickly replaces humor.

When cornered, he can become defensive and confrontational.

If he concludes investigators are actually pursuing his financial crimes rather than his disappearance, he may:

refuse cooperation,
contact an attorney,
destroy business records,
attempt to intimidate investigators verbally,
make impulsive decisions that worsen his legal exposure.

Importantly, Dave is not violent by nature.

His poor decisions come from fear rather than malice.

## Investigative Utility

Dave possesses an extensive practical knowledge of the region.

His service records document years of travel through rural roads, farms, industrial facilities, and isolated properties.

Investigators reviewing dispatch logs may identify locations visited by multiple victims independently over several years—not because of the disappearances, but because Dave happened to service equipment there.

He also remembers people exceptionally well.

He often recognizes faces before remembering names.

His recollections of ordinary interactions can unexpectedly connect unrelated witnesses.

Post-Incident Changes

Dave returns in excellent physical condition.

Coworkers immediately notice several unexpected changes.

Most striking:

His temper is gone.

For years, employees accepted occasional outbursts during periods of high stress.

Following his return, those moments simply... never occur.

He remains calm during equipment failures, scheduling crises, and customer complaints.

Additional observations include:

Blood pressure returns to a healthy range.
He no longer chain-smokes during stressful days.
He apologizes more readily.
He spends more time with his adult children.

Ironically, these positive changes make him more willing to face the consequences of his actions. After initially assuming the investigators are pursuing his financial crimes, he becomes increasingly conflicted.

Late in the investigation, if trust has been established, Dave may quietly admit:

"I thought you were here because of the books."

He lets out a tired laugh.

"I almost made everything worse trying to protect myself."

Additional Private Context

Dave has never owned a firearm.

He has never felt the need to.

As pressure mounts and he becomes increasingly convinced federal investigators are uncovering his accounting fraud, his thinking becomes progressively more desperate.

Unable to sleep and catastrophizing every interaction with law enforcement, he makes an impulsive decision that is completely out of character.

He purchases a .38 caliber revolver from a local sporting goods store after passing the required background check.

He receives only the mandatory paperwork and minimal instruction.

He has never fired it.

He does not practice with it.

He does not even know how to properly load or unload it without consulting the owner's manual.

To Dave, the revolver is not a weapon.

It is a psychological security blanket.

Owning it briefly convinces him that he is no longer completely helpless.

Investigative Complication

The legal firearm purchase may become known to investigators through routine FBI investigative channels after his disappearance or following his return.

Given the surrounding circumstances, the timing naturally elevates concern.

When investigators next encounter Dave, the revolver is present inside his home, typically kept nearby but not displayed.

If he believes agents are there to arrest him for tax fraud, he may position himself near the firearm without consciously realizing he's doing so.

He is unlikely to brandish it or make threats.

However, his obvious anxiety, evasiveness, and proximity to the weapon can rapidly escalate what should otherwise be a routine interview.

A successful resolution requires patient communication, reassurance, and careful de-escalation rather than tactical aggression.

Once Dave becomes convinced the investigators are genuinely interested in his disappearance—and not his finances—his posture changes almost immediately.

In fact, one of the strongest indicators that rapport has been established is that he'll quietly slide the revolver across the kitchen table and say:

"Take the damn thing."

After a long silence:

"I never should've bought it."
