# PER-13 — Rebecca Lawson

**LOCKED CANONICAL**

> Status: LOCKED CANONICAL

Rebecca Lawson
Basic Information

Age: 44

Occupation: High School Science Teacher

Relationship: Daughter of Harold "Hal" Whitaker

Residence: Syracuse, New York

Married.

One teenage son.

Visits her father nearly every weekend.

Public Overview

Rebecca inherited her father's curiosity and patience.

She teaches physics and earth science and credits Hal with inspiring her love of science from an early age.

Their relationship remained unusually close throughout adulthood.

After her mother's death several years earlier, Rebecca became her father's primary source of family support.

She worries about him constantly but respects his independence.

Personality

Calm.

Thoughtful.

Empathetic.

Naturally inquisitive.

Rebecca approaches problems the way a teacher approaches difficult questions—with patience rather than certainty.

She dislikes speculation unsupported by evidence.

Relationship with Hal

Rebecca describes her father as brilliant, kind, and endlessly curious.

Even in retirement he remained busy.

His interests included:

amateur radio,
astronomy,
electronics,
restoring old equipment,
mentoring younger operators.

She says he never truly retired.

"He just stopped getting paid."

After her mother's death, amateur radio became one of the few hobbies that consistently brought him joy.

Interview Guide
What Rebecca Knows
Hal attended amateur radio club meetings regularly.
He frequently mentored newer operators, especially Samantha Carter.
He enjoyed helping Nate Collins solve unusual radio problems.
He occasionally visited Chris Donnelly's workshop to collaborate on equipment repairs.
He spoke highly of nearly everyone in the club.

Rebecca always viewed the amateur radio community as an extended family for her father.

What Rebecca Doesn't Know

Rebecca has no knowledge of:

the Unnatural,
Delta Green,
Mi-Go,
classified investigations,
any hidden activities involving her father.

She believes his disappearance is completely inexplicable.

Behavioral Observations

A successful HUMINT or Psychology roll reveals:

Rebecca is less frightened than she is bewildered.

She repeatedly returns to one impossible observation.

"He stopped grieving."

She immediately clarifies.

"Not because he healed."

A long pause.

"Because it was... gone."

She becomes visibly uncomfortable after saying this aloud.

If Asked About Her Mother's Death

Rebecca explains that her father mourned deeply for several years after becoming widowed.

She watched him struggle through:

loneliness,
disrupted sleep,
depression,
anniversaries,
holidays.

Then, over the course of several weeks before his disappearance...

...all of it vanished.

Not gradually.

Simply...

gone.

Initially she celebrated the change.

Now she isn't sure what to think.

If Asked About His Mental State

Rebecca firmly rejects any suggestion that Hal suffered from dementia.

His memory remained exceptional.

He was intellectually sharp until the day he disappeared.

If anything, she felt he had become more energetic than he had been in years.

If Asked About His Last Conversation

Rebecca remembers speaking with him by telephone two nights before he disappeared.

He sounded unusually cheerful.

Before hanging up he said:

"I think I'm finally learning how to enjoy being old."

At the time it sounded hopeful.

Now she replays the sentence repeatedly, wondering whether it meant something else.

Investigative Value

Rebecca provides investigators with:

Context regarding Hal's life after becoming widowed.
Independent confirmation that his emotional state changed dramatically before his disappearance.
Family history.
Medical history.
Additional connections between Hal and the amateur radio community.

Her testimony is the strongest evidence that the victims' behavioral changes cannot be explained solely as improvements in mood.

Something fundamental about Hal's inner life had changed.

## Handler Notes

Rebecca's interview introduces one of the central themes of Episode 2:

The horror is not that people became happier. The horror is that something else decided what should be removed.

Unlike Jennifer, who speaks about positive changes in general terms, Rebecca can describe exactly what disappeared from her father's personality because she watched him grieve for years.

That makes her testimony emotionally powerful without becoming melodramatic. She isn't mourning only her missing father—she's struggling with the realization that, shortly before he vanished, the grief that connected him to the memory of her mother simply ceased to exist. She doesn't know whether to be grateful for that gift or terrified by it, and neither do the investigators.

I think this version makes Rebecca one of the most emotionally affecting supporting interviews in the operation. She gives the players their clearest glimpse into the ethical heart of the mystery: if the Unnatural can erase suffering, what else can it erase?
