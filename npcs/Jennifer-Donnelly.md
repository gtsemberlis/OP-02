# Jennifer Donnelly

> Status: LOCKED CANONICAL

Jennifer Donnelly
Basic Information

Age: 40

Occupation: Registered Nurse

Relationship: Wife of Christopher Donnelly

Residence: New Hartford, New York

Married to Chris for seventeen years.

Mother of two children.

Public Overview

Jennifer is practical, compassionate, and emotionally resilient.

Years of working as a nurse have made her comfortable around illness, crisis, and uncertainty.

She is not prone to dramatic conclusions.

When Chris disappears, she initially assumes there is an ordinary explanation.

As days become weeks, that certainty gradually erodes.

Personality

Jennifer is observant without being suspicious.

She listens carefully before speaking.

She values routine and stability.

Although deeply worried about her husband, she remains cooperative with investigators throughout the case.

She appreciates honesty and becomes frustrated when officials appear to withhold information.

Relationship with Chris

Jennifer describes Chris as dependable, quiet, and happiest when solving practical problems.

Their marriage was stable.

No known affairs.

No significant financial disputes.

No history of domestic violence.

She recalls that, during the months before his disappearance, Chris actually seemed happier than he had been in years.

He had:

quit smoking,
been sleeping better,
become more engaged with their children,
smiled more often.

At the time she considered these positive changes.

In retrospect, they now trouble her.

Interview Guide
What Jennifer Knows
Chris attended amateur radio club meetings regularly.
He enjoyed repairing equipment in his home workshop.
He occasionally helped other club members with electronics projects.
He had recently become friends with several operators from the club, including Hal Whitaker and Nate Collins.
Samantha Carter had visited the workshop once or twice as part of a club project, always with other members present.

Jennifer never observed anything inappropriate about those relationships.

What Jennifer Doesn't Know

Jennifer has no knowledge of:

Mi-Go activity,
the disappearances of the other victims before they became public,
Delta Green,
any unusual experiments,
any hidden personal life.

She genuinely believes Chris had nothing to hide.

Behavioral Observations

A successful HUMINT or Psychology roll reveals:

Jennifer's grief is mixed with confusion rather than anger.

She repeatedly returns to one observation:

"He was getting better."

When asked to explain, she struggles.

"People don't disappear when life is finally starting to feel... normal."

She finds this contradiction deeply unsettling.

If Asked About Chris's Mental Health

Jennifer firmly rejects the idea that Chris was suicidal.

She never observed:

depression,
hopelessness,
reckless behavior,
substance abuse.

She instead describes someone who seemed increasingly optimistic.

If Asked About His Last Day

Jennifer recalls nothing unusual.

Chris left home exactly as he normally would.

No argument.

No unusual phone calls.

No signs he intended not to return.

Her strongest memory is painfully mundane:

He reminded their youngest child to finish a science project before leaving.

Investigative Value

Jennifer helps investigators eliminate several conventional explanations by confirming:

stable home life,
healthy marriage,
improving emotional well-being,
no obvious motive to disappear voluntarily,
no evidence of criminal associations.

She also provides the first firsthand account that Chris's personality had begun changing before his disappearance, though neither she nor the investigators initially recognize its significance.

Handler Notes

Jennifer should never feel like a source of clues.

She is a grieving spouse trying to help.

The emotional weight of her interview comes from its normality. She offers no conspiracies, no dramatic revelations, and no hidden secrets—only the quiet certainty that something about Chris had changed for the better shortly before he vanished.

That ordinary observation becomes profoundly disturbing once the investigators begin hearing the same story from the families of the other victims.
