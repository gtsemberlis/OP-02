## Public Record

Elena Alvarez was born in the Bronx, New York, to Puerto Rican parents.

She earned a Bachelor's Degree in Psychology from the University at Albany before completing a Master's Degree in Criminal Justice.

Following several years as a state investigator, she joined the FBI.

Assignments have included:

Violent Crimes
Crimes Against Children
Behavioral Analysis support
Major Case Coordination

She eventually transferred to the Albany Field Office, where she now supervises investigations involving serial offenders, multi-jurisdictional disappearances, and complex violent crime.

Her personnel file reflects consistently excellent evaluations.

No disciplinary history.

Known for meticulous case management and exceptionally strong interview skills.

## Biography

Elena entered law enforcement because she believed every unanswered question represented someone still waiting for justice.

Her early career exposed her to some of the worst crimes ordinary people commit.

Those experiences left her compassionate but difficult to shock.

She became known for coordinating investigations involving multiple agencies, recognizing that information often fails to move between organizations more than investigators fail to discover it.

By the time the disappearances in Oneida County begin attracting federal attention, Elena has developed a reputation for bringing structure to investigations that appear increasingly chaotic.

## Private Context

Elena keeps remarkably little of her work at home.

She intentionally separates her professional and personal life.

The exception is unsolved cases.

She occasionally revisits old investigative notebooks, asking herself whether overlooked details might now appear differently.

Her divorce stemmed largely from the demands of federal investigations rather than conflict or betrayal.

She remains on good terms with her former spouse but privately wonders whether meaningful personal relationships are compatible with her career.

Cognitive Signature

Excellent integrator of information.

Rarely becomes attached to a single theory.

Constantly reevaluates assumptions.

Highly disciplined in distinguishing evidence from interpretation.

Recognizes patterns across investigations that others often view independently.

Comfortable admitting uncertainty.

## Communication Style

Warm without being informal.

Direct.

Patient.

Excellent listener.

## Under Pressure

Becomes more methodical.

Reduces speculation.

Expands documentation.

Actively seeks dissenting opinions before committing investigative resources.

If evidence contradicts her conclusions, she changes her conclusions.

Never the reverse.

Operational & Mechanical Notes

Elena is not initially aware of Delta Green's involvement.

She expects to lead a conventional federal investigation into a possible serial offender or organized criminal enterprise.

Once Delta Green identifies her as the federal case agent, she becomes their primary point of contact within the FBI.

She never knowingly violates FBI policy.

Instead, she quietly accepts that some investigations occasionally require information to move through channels she cannot officially acknowledge.

She asks very few questions she suspects will never receive honest answers.

Exposure

Eight years earlier, Elena coordinated a joint FBI and state investigation into a series of disappearances in the Adirondack High Peaks.

Search teams repeatedly reported impossible radio interference, contradictory GPS tracks, and witnesses describing locations that could not later be found.

One search-and-rescue volunteer returned after being missing for nearly forty-eight hours with no memory of where he had been. His equipment recorded environmental conditions that could not be reconciled with any known location in the region.

The case was abruptly transferred to another federal authority.

Elena was instructed to archive her findings and cease further inquiry.

Several weeks later, she received a discreet visit from a man who identified himself only as representing another part of the government.

He did not explain what had happened.

He simply told her:

"You asked the right questions, documented the evidence honestly, and stopped when you reached the edge of what you could prove."

Before leaving, he handed her a business card with no name and one instruction:

"If you ever find another case where the evidence refuses to fit the world you know... call this number before you call anyone else."

She has used that number exactly once.

This operation will be the second.

Design Notes

Making Elena a Friendly rather than a Program Agent keeps her firmly grounded in the ordinary world. She is still an FBI supervisor first and foremost. Delta Green values her not because she believes in the Unnatural, but because she demonstrates the qualities the Program depends on: intellectual honesty, discretion, professional competence, and the humility to recognize when a case has exceeded conventional explanation.

Her relationship with the Program is built on trust, not membership, making her an ideal bridge between the federal investigation and the agents without overshadowing either. I think she'll become one of those NPCs that players instinctively trust—not because she's "in on the secret," but because she's earned it.
